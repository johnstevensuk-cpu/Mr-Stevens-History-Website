What is History? How Do We Study It?

🔍 What is History? How Do We Study It?

Before studying any event, we must understand HOW historians work. History is asking questions about the past, finding evidence, evaluating it critically, and constructing justified arguments. The Bayeux Tapestry (above left, c.1077) is a perfect primary source — but who made it, for whom, and with what purpose? The Domesday Book (above right, 1086) is another: William's comprehensive land survey. Historians always apply WACA. We also work with CHRONOLOGY (ordering events in time), CAUSATION (why things happened) and CONSEQUENCE (what resulted).

Key method: WACA — Who, Audience, Context, AimKey skill: Chronology — BC counts BACKWARDS, AD forwards

The Norman Conquest 1066

⚔️ The Norman Conquest 1066

In 1066, the death of King Edward the Confessor without an heir triggered a succession crisis. Three men claimed the throne: Harold Godwinson, Harald Hardrada of Norway, and William, Duke of Normandy. Harold defeated Hardrada at Stamford Bridge on 25 September but had to march 250 miles south immediately. At Hastings on 14 October 1066, Harold was killed. The Bayeux Tapestry (above left, c.1077) records these events — apply WACA to it! The Domesday Book (above right, 1086) shows how William consolidated control through a comprehensive land survey — an administrative revolution that transformed England's governance.

Medieval Entertainment & Life

🎭 Medieval Entertainment & Life

Bruegel's 'Peasant Dance' (above, c.1568) shows medieval-style communal entertainment: dancing, feasting and communal games. Remember WACA: Bruegel painted this in 1568 — over 300 years after the medieval period. Does this matter? Holy days (the origin of 'holiday') were the only breaks from agricultural labour. Mob football — played between whole villages with no rules — was banned by Edward III in 1363. Minstrels brought news and stories. Sumptuary laws actually prohibited lower classes from wearing certain fabrics and colours, revealing the deep inequalities of medieval society.

1363 Edward III bans mob footballKey term: Sumptuary laws

Health, Medicine & The Black Death

☠️ Health, Medicine & The Black Death

The 'Danse Macabre' by Wolgemut (above, c.1493) shows Death taking victims from every social class. Apply WACA: this was made 145 years after the Black Death — what does that mean for its usefulness as evidence? The Black Death arrived in England in 1348, killing 30–50% of the population within two years. Medieval medicine was completely powerless. Physicians used Galen's 1,500-year-old theory of four humours. The three main explanations were: God's punishment, bad air (miasma theory), or planetary alignment. Long-term: labour shortages gave peasants extraordinary new bargaining power — the feudal system began to collapse.

Castles — Motte & Bailey to Stone Behemoths

🏰 Castles — Motte & Bailey to Stone Behemoths

The Tower of London (above) — begun by William around 1078 — is the most powerful surviving symbol of Norman conquest, with walls 3–4 metres thick and standing 27 metres high. Apply WACA: this building itself is a historical source. After 1066, William built castles at extraordinary speed. Motte-and-bailey castles — a wooden tower on a raised earthen mound (motte), with an enclosed yard (bailey) — could be built in days using forced local labour. They evolved into magnificent stone keeps over the following century. Castles served as military strongholds, symbols of Norman power, administrative centres, and residences for the lord's household.

c.1066–87 William builds hundreds of castlesc.1078 Tower of London begun

The Transatlantic Slave Trade

⛓️ The Transatlantic Slave Trade

The Brookes diagram (above left, 1787) and the Triangular Trade map (above right) together tell the story of one of history's greatest crimes. WACA the Brookes: it was produced by abolitionists — who made it? For what purpose? The Brookes showed 609 enslaved people packed into spaces barely 50 centimetres high. West Africa was home to sophisticated kingdoms — Ashanti, Dahomey, Benin — before European colonisation. The abolition campaign was led by Equiano, Wilberforce and Clarkson. Britain abolished the slave trade in 1807 and slavery itself in 1833.

🏭 Industrial Revolution — Farms to Factories

Loutherbourg's 'Coalbrookdale by Night' (above, 1801) shows furnaces blazing through the night — WACA it! Between 1750 and 1900, Britain became the world's first industrialised nation. Manchester grew from 25,000 in 1772 to over 300,000 by 1850. The Sadler Report of 1833 exposed shocking child labour conditions, driving Parliamentary reform. This chain — Industrial Revolution → urbanisation → cholera → public health reform → Beveridge Report (1942) → NHS (1948) — represents one of history's most important long-term consequences.

Victorian Living Conditions

🏙️ Victorian Living Conditions & Public Health

The rapid growth of industrial cities created catastrophic public health crises. Overcrowded back-to-back housing, open sewers, contaminated water and overflowing cesspits created perfect conditions for cholera. Dr John Snow's mapping of the 1854 Soho cholera outbreak — tracing all deaths to a single contaminated pump on Broad Street — proved water (not miasma) caused cholera: a landmark in scientific detective work. The Great Stink of 1858, when the Thames became so polluted it drove Parliament out of Westminster, finally forced investment in Bazalgette's revolutionary sewage system.

1832,48,54 Major cholera outbreaks1854 John Snow maps cholera1858 The Great Stink

🌏 British Empire & Age of Exploration

The 'Imperial Federation' map of 1886 (above) shows British territories in red — one quarter of the world's land surface. WACA this map: Who made it? When? For whom? What message does it send? The East India Company gradually assumed military control over India. The Indian Rebellion of 1857 — triggered by military grievances, cultural insensitivity and economic exploitation — led to direct British Crown rule (the Raj), with Victoria becoming Empress of India in 1876. The Empire's legacy remains deeply contested: infrastructure and trade co-existed with racism, exploitation and the suppression of local cultures.

Year 7 — The Norman Conquest 1066

The Domesday Book

📍 Source: The Domesday Book, 1086 (commissioned by William the Conqueror)
The Domesday Book was a comprehensive survey of all land and property in England, commissioned by William I in 1085 and completed in 1086. It recorded who owned what, how much it was worth, and how many people lived and worked on each estate. It was called 'Domesday' because its judgements were as final as the Day of Judgement.
"Here is set down how much land the King himself holds in England, and how much his barons hold, how many knights, how many peasants, how many villeins, how many freemen, how many slaves, what each holds, and how much livestock, and how much each manor is worth in the time of King Edward, and how much it is worth now."

Aneurin (Nye) Bevan

📍 Source: Aneurin (Nye) Bevan, Speech at the formal launch of the National Health Service, 5 July 1948
Aneurin Bevan was the Minister of Health in Clement Attlee's Labour government. He was the architect and driving force behind the creation of the NHS. He delivered this speech at the Park Hospital in Manchester (now Trafford General) on the official launch day of the NHS, 5 July 1948 — the day the first NHS patient was treated. He had overcome fierce opposition from the British Medical Association to reach this point.
"The NHS will last as long as there are folk left with faith to fight for it. It has not had an easy passage. There have been enormous difficulties. It was my job to overcome these difficulties. We now have the moral leadership of the world."
