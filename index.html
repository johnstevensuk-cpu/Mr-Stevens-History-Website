📅 Year 7 · Medieval England · Topic 2 · 16 Lessons

🎭 Medieval
Entertainment & Life

For most medieval people, entertainment was tightly bound to the Church calendar. Holy days gave peasants rare free time for games, sport and feasting — while sumptuary laws enforced the class divide. After 1066, Old English and Norman French blended to create the language we speak today.

📜 WACA Source Challenge — Bruegel's Peasant Dance (c.1568)
"Peasant Dance" by Pieter Bruegel the Elder (c.1568) — communal village celebrations: dancing, feasting, music and games. Public domain.
W
Pieter Bruegel the Elder — Dutch painter. NOT English. NOT from the medieval period.
A
Wealthy Dutch art collectors and church patrons — not English peasants.
C
c.1568 — Dutch Renaissance. 200 years AFTER the Black Death. NOT the medieval period itself.
A
To romanticise peasant/village life — possibly nostalgic. NOT an objective record.
Key question: Can we use a painting made in 1568 as evidence of medieval life from 1250–1400? What can it tell us? What can it NOT tell us?
Write your WACA judgement: "This source is useful for understanding [X] because... however it is limited because..."

📜 Primary Source — King Edward III, Royal Proclamation, 1363

📜 Banning Mob Football in London
"Whereas there is great noise in the city caused by hustling over large balls, from which many evils might arise, we command and forbid, on behalf of the king, on pain of imprisonment, such game to be used in the city in future."
W
King Edward III — monarch. Issued as a royal proclamation.
A
Citizens of London, sheriffs — those responsible for enforcement.
C
1363 — England at war with France. Archery practice was essential.
A
To ban football AND ensure men practise archery for war instead.
Q1. What does this source tell us about what authorities thought of mob football? Quote evidence directly.
Q2. WACA: Who issued this? When? Who was it aimed at? What was its purpose?
Q3. Why might this source be useful — and what does it NOT tell us about how ordinary people felt about football?

🎯 Activities — Bronze to Platinum

🎭 Activity 1: Medieval Entertainment Programme

You are the royal entertainment organiser for a Norman castle feast in 1250. Plan a historically accurate full-day programme from dawn to midnight.

  • Create a timed programme including at least SIX different activities
  • For each activity: who could participate — nobles only, peasants, or everyone — and WHY social class affected access
  • Mark any activities banned or restricted by the Church and explain why
  • Compare: which of these activities still exist today in some form?
⚖️ Activity 2: Sumptuary Laws Investigation
  • What were sumptuary laws? Why did medieval rulers create them?
  • Look at the Bruegel painting above — can you identify clues about social status from clothing and activities?
  • What colours and fabrics were reserved for nobles only? Why did this matter?
  • Challenge: Write a sumptuary law proclamation in the style of a medieval king — what would you ban and why?
🗣️ Activity 3: The Story of English

Using your Story of English lesson resource:

  • What happened to the English language after 1066? Why did it change?
  • Find FIVE modern English words that came from Norman French
  • Find FIVE modern English words that kept their Old English roots
  • Why do we have TWO words for the same thing (e.g. 'pig/pork', 'cow/beef')? What does this tell us about Norman society?

🔍 What is History?
& The Norman Conquest 1066

In 1066, the death of King Edward the Confessor triggered a succession crisis. Three men claimed the throne. Harold won at Stamford Bridge — but had to march 250 miles south immediately. At Hastings on 14 October 1066, Harold was killed and William was victorious.

☠️ Health, Medicine
& The Black Death

The Black Death arrived in England in 1348, killing 30–50% of the population within two years. Medieval medicine was completely powerless. Long-term: labour shortages gave surviving peasants extraordinary new bargaining power — and the feudal system began to collapse.

🏰 Castles — Motte & Bailey
to Stone Behemoths

After 1066, William built castles at remarkable speed. The Tower of London (begun c.1078) is the most powerful symbol of Norman conquest. By 1100 there were over 500 castles in England. Bronze to Platinum differentiated research project.
