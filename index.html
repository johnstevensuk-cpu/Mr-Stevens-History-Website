What is History? How Do We Study It?

🔍 What is History? How Do We Study It?

Before studying any event we must understand HOW historians work. History is asking questions about the past, finding evidence, evaluating it critically and constructing justified arguments. The Bayeux Tapestry (above left, c.1077) is a perfect primary source — but who made it, for whom, with what purpose? Apply WACA! The Domesday Book (above right, 1086) is another: William's comprehensive land survey. WACA = Who created it? For what Audience? In what Context? With what Aim?

Key method: WACA — Who, Audience, Context, AimKey skill: Chronology — BC counts BACKWARDS, AD forwards

The Norman Conquest 1066

⚔️ The Norman Conquest 1066

In 1066 the death of King Edward the Confessor triggered a succession crisis. Three men claimed the throne: Harold Godwinson, Harald Hardrada, and William, Duke of Normandy. Harold defeated Hardrada at Stamford Bridge on 25 September — but had to march 250 miles south immediately. At Hastings on 14 October 1066, Harold was killed. The Bayeux Tapestry (above left) records these events — apply WACA! The Domesday Book (above right, 1086) shows how William then consolidated control.

Health, Medicine & The Black Death

☠️ Health, Medicine & The Black Death

The 'Danse Macabre' by Wolgemut (above, c.1493) — WACA it immediately: made 145 years AFTER the 1348 Black Death. The Black Death arrived in England in 1348, killing 30–50% of the population. Medieval medicine was completely powerless. Physicians used Galen's 1,500-year-old theory of four humours. The three main explanations: God's punishment, bad air (miasma theory), or planetary alignment. Long-term: labour shortages gave peasants extraordinary new bargaining power.

🏙️ Victorian Living Conditions & Public Health

Dr John Snow's mapping of the 1854 Soho cholera outbreak — tracing all deaths to a contaminated pump on Broad Street — proved water caused cholera: a landmark in scientific detective work. The Great Stink of 1858, when the Thames became so polluted it drove MPs out of Parliament, finally forced investment in Bazalgette's sewers.
