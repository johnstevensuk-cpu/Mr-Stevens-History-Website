What is History? How Do We Study It?

History is not about memorising dates — it is about asking questions, weighing evidence, and building justified arguments. Before studying any event we must understand HOW historians work. Think of yourself as a detective: every source is a clue, every event has a cause, and every story has more than one side.

🧠 WACA Source Analysis

Apply WACA to every source before judging its usefulness:

  • W — Who created this source? What do we know about them?
  • A — For what Audience was it created?
  • C — In what Context was it made? What was happening at the time?
  • A — What was the creator's Aim? What were they trying to achieve?

Example: The Bayeux Tapestry was commissioned by Bishop Odo of Bayeux — William's half-brother. Apply WACA fully!

🔍 Primary vs Secondary Sources
  • PRIMARY = created AT THE TIME of the event
  • SECONDARY = created AFTER using primary sources
  • Classify: Bayeux Tapestry (c.1077), a 2024 BBC documentary, a WW1 soldier's letter, your history textbook
  • Can a primary source ever be LESS reliable than a secondary one? Give an example.
⚖️ Fact vs Opinion
  • FACT = can be proven. OPINION = belief or judgement.
  • 'William won Hastings on 14 Oct 1066' = FACT ✅
  • 'William was the greatest king' = OPINION 💭
  • Find 4 facts and 4 opinions from your lesson resources. Explain why distinguishing them matters.

The Norman Conquest 1066

In 1066 the death of King Edward the Confessor triggered a succession crisis. Three men claimed the throne: Harold Godwinson, Harald Hardrada, and William, Duke of Normandy. Harold defeated Hardrada at Stamford Bridge on 25 September — but had to march 250 miles south immediately. At Hastings on 14 October 1066, Harold was killed.

Medieval Entertainment & Life

Holy days gave peasants rare free time for games, sport and feasting. The Church dominated every aspect of life from birth to death. Mob football was banned by Edward III in 1363 — it was distracting men from archery practice needed for war. Sumptuary laws prohibited lower classes from wearing certain fabrics.

Health, Medicine & The Black Death

The Black Death arrived in England in 1348, killing 30–50% of the population within two years. Medieval medicine was completely powerless — physicians used Galen's 1,500-year-old theory of four humours. The three main explanations: God's punishment, bad air (miasma theory), or planetary alignment. Long-term: labour shortages gave peasants extraordinary new bargaining power.

Slavery & the Slave Trade

The transatlantic slave trade forcibly transported over 12 million Africans to the Americas over 400 years. West African kingdoms (Ashanti, Dahomey, Benin) had sophisticated economies before European colonisation. Britain abolished the slave trade in 1807 and slavery itself in 1833.

Farms to Factories — The Industrial Revolution

Britain transformed from an agricultural society to an industrial powerhouse between 1750–1900. Manchester grew from 25,000 in 1772 to 316,000 by 1851. The Sadler Report of 1833 exposed shocking child labour conditions. This chain — Industrialisation → urbanisation → cholera → reforms → Beveridge → NHS — is one of history's most important long-term causal chains.

Victorian Living Conditions & Public Health

Victorian cities grew explosively but without planning. Overcrowding, open sewers, contaminated water, and cholera epidemics killed thousands. Dr John Snow's mapping of the 1854 Soho cholera outbreak proved water caused cholera — a landmark in scientific detective work. The Great Stink of 1858 finally forced investment in Bazalgette's revolutionary sewage system.

Nelson & The Battle of Trafalgar 1805

Admiral Horatio Nelson became Britain's greatest naval hero at the Battle of Trafalgar (21 October 1805). His unconventional two-column attack broke the Franco-Spanish formation and allowed British ships to fight at close quarters. Britain won without losing a single ship. Nelson died at 4:30pm.

Cold War Source — Churchill's Iron Curtain Speech, 1946

📊 7–8 / 8 marks · Grade 9

'How useful is Churchill's Iron Curtain speech (March 1946) for understanding the origins of the Cold War?' [8 marks]

Content + QuotesChurchill's speech is useful as it directly describes the key change that led to Cold War tensions: 'an iron curtain has descended across the Continent'. This shows that Churchill identified Soviet control over Eastern Europe as a critical threat, naming specific capitals — 'Warsaw, Berlin, Prague, Vienna, Budapest' — demonstrating how geographically widespread Soviet influence had become by March 1946.
WACA ProvenanceHowever, the source's usefulness is limited by its provenance. Churchill was no longer Prime Minister, having lost the 1945 election — yet President Truman sat on the platform beside him. This semi-official backing suggests Churchill was not simply a private citizen, but his personal political motivation to re-engage the USA in European affairs must be considered. The audience was predominantly American, which means Churchill may have exaggerated Soviet control to alarm US policymakers.
Own KnowledgeOwn knowledge confirms that Soviet control was genuine and growing: by 1946 Stalin had installed communist governments in Poland and Romania, and would soon extend control to Czechoslovakia (1948) and blockade Berlin (1948-49). Churchill's phrase 'iron curtain' was not an exaggeration — it accurately described what was happening across Eastern Europe.
JudgementOverall, this source is highly useful for understanding how contemporaries perceived the origins of the Cold War. Churchill's description of Soviet control is confirmed by subsequent events. However, his aim of alarming American public opinion means we should treat his language as simultaneously evidence AND propaganda — which itself tells us much about how Western leaders understood the Soviet threat.

Vietnam War — 16-Mark Extended Essay

📊 14–15 / 16 marks · Grade 9

'The USA lost in Vietnam primarily because of what happened at HOME, not on the battlefield.' How far do you agree? [16 marks]

Introduction — ThesisThe USA's failure in Vietnam resulted from a combination of factors — home front opposition, military tactical failures and Vietcong resilience — but the argument that the home front was the PRIMARY cause is partially convincing. While the Tet Offensive undoubtedly shattered American public confidence, the underlying military situation was already deteriorating before the home front collapsed.
Paragraph 1 — Home FrontThe collapse of home front support was decisive. The Tet Offensive (January 1968) was a military defeat for the Vietcong — US and South Vietnamese forces repelled all attacks — but its political consequences were catastrophic. Walter Cronkite, trusted by 73% of Americans, declared the war 'a stalemate' on 27 February 1968. President Johnson's approval rating fell below 36%, and he announced he would not seek re-election. The anti-war movement grew dramatically: from Kent State (May 1970) to widespread draft resistance. Without public support, the government could not maintain the political will to continue.
Paragraph 2 — Battlefield FactorsHowever, battlefield factors were equally significant. The Vietcong's guerrilla tactics — the 250km Cu Chi tunnel network, hit-and-run raids, booby traps, and their ability to blend with civilians — made US military superiority largely irrelevant. Search and Destroy operations (Operation Rolling Thunder from 1965) dropped more bombs on Vietnam than by all sides combined in WW2, yet failed to break Vietnamese resistance. Strategic Hamlet Programme alienated the South Vietnamese population whose support was essential. The US was fighting the wrong kind of war with the wrong tactics.
Paragraph 3 — ComparisonComparing both factors, the home front collapse was itself a consequence of battlefield failure. Had the USA been winning decisively on the battlefield, public opinion would not have turned. Conversely, the Vietcong's resilience was partly explained by Vietnamese nationalism — Ho Chi Minh's forces were 'fighting for our home' as one veteran noted — which US military power simply could not overcome regardless of public opinion at home. The two factors are inseparable.
ConclusionOn balance, the statement is partially valid but oversimplified. The home front collapse (catalysed by Tet) was the immediate cause of US withdrawal — without it, the war might have continued longer. But the FUNDAMENTAL reason the USA lost was that it could not achieve military victory against a determined nationalist guerrilla force on difficult terrain with local support. The home front was the point at which an already-failing military campaign became politically unsustainable. The USA did not lose because of what happened at home — it lost at home because it had already lost militarily.

Stalin's Russia — 16-Mark Extended Essay

📊 13–14 / 16 marks · Grade 8

'Terror was the most important method Stalin used to maintain power in the Soviet Union.' How far do you agree? [16 marks]

IntroductionStalin used two broad methods to maintain power: terror (the NKVD, Gulag and show trials) and propaganda (the cult of personality, censorship and Stakhanovism). The argument that terror was the most important method is substantially convincing, but ultimately both were necessary and complementary — neither would have been sufficient alone.
Paragraph 1 — TerrorTerror was certainly extensive and systematic. The NKVD arrested approximately 1.5 million people during the Great Terror (1936-38), with at least 750,000 executed. The Gulag at its peak (1953) held 1.8 million people in 476 camp complexes across Siberia. Show trials — such as Zinoviev-Kamenev (1936) and Bukharin (1938) — forced defendants to confess to crimes they did not commit through torture, family threats and psychological manipulation, publicly humiliating potential rivals and warning the population of the consequences of disloyalty.
Paragraph 2 — PropagandaYet propaganda was equally essential. Stalin's official portrait (c.1930s) — showing him in plain military uniform, calm and authoritative, with an upward-directed gaze suggesting vision and leadership — was produced in enormous quantities and displayed in every office, school and factory. Citizens were expected to display reverence. The Stakhanovite movement (from 1935) celebrated 'heroic workers' who exceeded production quotas, creating the impression of a prosperous, enthusiastic society. Censorship ensured no alternative narrative could reach the population. The cult of personality genuinely convinced millions — not merely through fear but through manufactured belief.
Paragraph 3 — ComparisonThe crucial point is the RELATIONSHIP between the two methods. Terror alone creates fear, but not the genuine loyalty or productive enthusiasm that Stalin's regime also needed. Soviet economic growth — steel production rising from 4 to 18 million tonnes 1928-1940 — required workers to genuinely produce, not just avoid punishment. Propaganda created the positive motivation that terror alone could not. Equally, propaganda without terror would have been insufficient: without the genuine threat of the Gulag, dissent would have been more easily expressed and organised.
ConclusionOn balance, terror and propaganda were equally important but served different functions: terror prevented opposition and created fear, while propaganda built positive legitimacy and manufactured consent. The statement is therefore partially true — terror was indispensable — but the more sophisticated answer is that both were required and that Stalin's genius, such as it was, lay in deploying both simultaneously and ruthlessly.

Norman Conquest — KS3 Extended Writing

📊 10–11 / 12 marks · Grade 8 equivalent

'Harold Godwinson's own mistakes were the most important reason why William won at Hastings.' How far do you agree? [12 marks equivalent]

IntroductionWilliam's victory at Hastings on 14 October 1066 resulted from a combination of Harold's tactical errors and William's strengths. The argument that Harold's mistakes were the most important cause is substantially supported by the evidence, though William's superior preparation and tactical innovation also played a critical role.
Paragraph 1 — Harold's MistakesHarold's most critical mistake was the decision to march south immediately after the exhausting victory at Stamford Bridge on 25 September 1066 — just 19 days before Hastings. The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle records that 'many men fell ill on the march and many remained behind.' Harold arrived with a depleted, exhausted army. His second mistake was fighting defensively on a ridge rather than maximising his numerical strength, and his third was failing to maintain the shield wall when the Norman cavalry feigned retreat — this disorder ultimately allowed Norman cavalry to break through.
Paragraph 2 — William's StrengthsHowever, William's role should not be underestimated. His tactical innovation — attacking in two columns perpendicular to Harold's line — broke conventional medieval battle formation. His cavalry were well-rested and professional, having had time to recover after landing at Pevensey on 28 September. The feigned retreat that drew Harold's housecarls from the ridge was a sophisticated planned tactic, not an accident. William had also prepared meticulously — unlike Harold, who had only weeks' notice.
ConclusionOn balance, Harold's mistakes were significant but cannot be fully separated from William's strengths. Had Harold not been forced to march south from Stamford Bridge, his army might have been equal to William's. But William's tactical skill — especially the feigned retreat — suggests he might have won even against a well-rested opponent. The most important factor was the timing of the two battles: Harold had 19 days between Stamford Bridge and Hastings, which was simply not enough. This was partly bad luck and partly Harold's own error of judgment in not waiting before engaging.

Changes in Medicine — 8-Mark Source Analysis

📊 6–7 / 8 marks · Grade 8–9

'How useful is Aneurin Bevan's NHS launch speech (5 July 1948) for understanding the creation of the NHS?' [8 marks]

Content + QuotesBevan's speech is useful as it directly demonstrates the confidence with which the NHS was launched. His claim that 'We now have the moral leadership of the world' reveals how radically transformative the NHS was perceived to be in 1948 — free healthcare at the point of need was genuinely unprecedented in scale. His reference to 'enormous difficulties' confirms that the NHS faced significant opposition, which own knowledge identifies as the British Medical Association's fierce campaign against it.
WACA ProvenanceHowever, Bevan was the NHS's own architect — the Minister of Health who had personally designed and fought for it against BMA opposition. His position as the source's creator critically limits its usefulness: this is a celebratory speech on launch day by the person most invested in the NHS's success. The audience was the launch ceremony at Park Hospital, Manchester, and the wider British public through media coverage. His aim — to celebrate the achievement, inspire supporters and establish his legacy — means the source inevitably presents only the positive case.
Own KnowledgeOwn knowledge partially challenges Bevan's triumphant narrative. The 'enormous difficulties' were largely the BMA's fierce opposition — GPs feared the NHS would make them salaried civil servants rather than independent professionals. Bevan overcame this by allowing hospital consultants to treat private patients in NHS hospitals ('stuffing their mouths with gold'). The NHS's success was also built on WW2 medical advances — particularly penicillin (developed from Fleming's 1928 discovery) and blood plasma — which Bevan does not mention.
JudgementOverall, this source is useful for understanding how contemporaries perceived the NHS's launch and Bevan's political ambitions, but it must be treated as a celebratory political speech rather than a balanced account. It tells us more about Bevan's confidence and aspirations than about the complex causes of the NHS's creation, which included Beveridge's 1942 report, Labour's 1945 election victory, WW2 medical advances, and the long history of public health reform from John Snow onwards.
