⏱️ Chronology — History's First Tool

Imagine watching a film where the scenes are completely out of order. That is what history looks like without chronology — the ability to place events in the correct sequence across time. Historians use two systems: BC (Before Christ) and AD (Anno Domini — "in the year of the Lord"). The crucial rule that trips everyone up: BC counts backwards. The year 500 BC is 400 years older than 100 BC.

📜 Primary vs Secondary Sources

📜 Primary Sources

Created at the time of the event — by someone who was there or had direct access to it.

Examples: The Bayeux Tapestry (c.1077) · A WW1 soldier's diary · A Tudor painting · A medieval law · A photograph taken at the scene
📚 Secondary Sources

Created after the event — using primary sources to draw conclusions. The person was not there.

Examples: Your history textbook · A 2024 documentary · An academic book · This website · A museum display

⚠️ Critical insight: A primary source is NOT automatically more reliable than a secondary one. An eyewitness can be biased, mistaken, or deliberately dishonest. A secondary source written by a careful historian may be far more accurate than a biased primary one. This is why we always apply WACA.

🔍 The WACA Framework — Unlocking Every Source

Before using any source as evidence, interrogate it. WACA gives you four questions that reveal a source's true value — and its hidden limitations.

W
WHO?
Who created this source? What was their role, background, and possible reasons for bias?
A
AUDIENCE?
Who was this made for? A private diary is very different from a public speech or propaganda poster.
C
CONTEXT?
When was it created? What was happening at that time that might have shaped what was included or left out?
A
AIM?
What was the creator trying to achieve? To persuade? Celebrate? Record for posterity? Cover something up?
✍️ The WACA Judgement — Write It Like This
"This source is useful for understanding [WHAT] because [WACA evidence]. However, it is limited because [counter-WACA point]."

Key principle: A biased source is NOT useless. Propaganda is valuable evidence of what someone WANTED people to believe.

⚖️ Fact, Opinion and Cause & Effect

Three more tools every historian needs before they can write about the past effectively.

✅
Historical FACT
Something that can be proven — it either happened or it didn't. Example: "William defeated Harold at the Battle of Hastings on 14 October 1066." This can be verified from multiple independent sources.
💭
Historical OPINION
A judgement or interpretation that cannot be proven beyond doubt. Example: "William was a better king than Harold would have been." Different historians may reach different conclusions from the same evidence.
⚡
CAUSE
What made an event happen. Causes can be long-term (building up over years) or short-term (the immediate trigger). Example: Harold's exhausted army = a CAUSE of his defeat at Hastings.
🌊
EFFECT
What happened as a result of an event. Effects can be immediate or long-term. Example: The Norman language transforming English = a long-term EFFECT of the Conquest.

🏛️ Why History Matters

The skills you develop in history — WACA, chronology, distinguishing fact from opinion, identifying causes and effects — are not just useful in exams. They are the skills of critical thinking: the ability to interrogate a source, question an argument, and reach a justified conclusion. In a world of social media, fake news, and political spin, these might be the most important skills you ever learn.

📜 E.H. Carr, 'What is History?', 1961
"History is an unending dialogue between the present and the past."

🎉 What Was a Holy Day?

The word "holiday" comes directly from "holy day" — and that single fact reveals everything about medieval leisure. Ordinary people had almost no free time. They worked from dawn to dusk for their lord, six or seven days a week. But on religious feast days — around 50 per year — they were briefly free. In those precious hours of freedom, medieval entertainment came to life.

  • Why was it important that holy days were religious celebrations — not just a day off? What did this mean for how people spent their time?

⚽ Mob Football — Banned Repeatedly, Played Regardless

Imagine football with no rules, no referee, no fixed team size, and goals sometimes several miles apart. That was mob football — and multiple kings banned it. People played it anyway, which itself tells us something important about the relationship between ordinary people and royal authority.

📋 The (Non-) Rules
  • ⚽ Ball: an inflated pig's bladder
  • 👥 Players: entire villages — sometimes hundreds on each side
  • 🗺️ Goals: could be miles apart in different villages
  • ⚖️ Rules: almost none — "anything short of murder"
  • 💀 Injuries and occasionally deaths were not uncommon
  • 📅 Usually played on Shrove Tuesday (pancake day)
🚫 Royal Bans — And Why They Failed
1314Edward II bans football in London — completely ignored
1331Edward III bans it — still ignored
1363Edward III bans it AGAIN — wants men practising archery
1667Still being banned. Still being played.
📜 Source A — William Fitzstephen, monk, c.1174 (first written description of football)
"After dinner, all the youth of the city go out into the fields to take part in the ball game. The students of each school have their own ball; the workers from each city craft are also carrying their balls."

Apply WACA: Fitzstephen was a Norman monk writing about London. What might he have left out about how violent the game was? What does 'youth of the city' suggest about who could participate?

📜 Source B — A royal proclamation banning football, 14th century
"Because of the great uproar in the city caused by the hustling over large balls, from which many evils might arise, we do forbid this game henceforth in the city."

Apply WACA: who issued this? Why do you think it uses the phrase 'many evils might arise'? What were the kings actually worried about?

🎵 Music, Minstrels, and Miracle Plays

Before recorded music, radio, or television, entertainment was entirely live — and the wandering minstrel was medieval England's answer to a streaming service. These professional performers travelled from castle to village to market town, earning their keep with songs, stories, news, acrobatics, and magic tricks. They were simultaneously entertainers, news anchors, and the social media of their age.

🎭 Kinds of Entertainment
  • 🎵 Minstrels — professional musicians telling stories in song
  • 🤹 Jugglers & acrobats — at fairs and on market days
  • 🎪 Mystery plays — religious dramas performed in town streets
  • 🐻 Bear-baiting — brutal but extremely popular with all classes
  • 🎲 Dice, chess, cards — for merchants, nobles, and clergy
  • 🏹 Archery contests — civic duty AND sport; kings encouraged them
🏆 Sports & Physical Games
  • ⛸️ Ice skating — on animal bones strapped to boots
  • 🎯 Bowls, quoits, stool-ball — forerunners of cricket and golf
  • 🐓 Cock-fighting — widespread; heavily gambled upon
  • 🐟 Fishing & hunting — hunting strictly reserved for the nobility
  • 🏇 Jousting tournaments — spectacular events for the knightly class
  • 🌰 Conkers — yes, medieval children played conkers too

👗 Fashion — What Your Clothes Said About You

In medieval England, your clothing was your identity badge. Everyone could tell at a glance exactly who you were, how wealthy you were, and where you stood in society — just by looking at what you wore. "Sumptuary laws" tried to prevent people from dressing above their station. Peasants in silk? Illegal. Merchants wearing fur? Technically a crime. And yet the laws were repeatedly broken — which itself tells us something very important.

👑
Royalty & High Nobility
Purple, ermine fur, silk brocade, gold thread, precious jewels. Purple dye cost more than gold per gram — it had to be imported from across the Mediterranean world.
⚔️
Knights & Gentry
Fine English wools, leather, decorated sword belts, quality boots. Limited fur trim. Bright colours from good English dyes. Clearly superior to peasant dress.
🌾
Peasants & Serfs
Rough undyed wool, coarse linen. Browns, greys, muddy greens — the natural colours of cheap, locally available dyes. No silk. No fur. No jewels. No colour.

🍽️ Food — A Tale of Two Very Different Tables

👑 The Noble's Great Feast
  • 🦢 Roast swan, peacock, venison, wild boar
  • 🌶️ Exotic spices imported from Asia: pepper, cinnamon, ginger, nutmeg
  • 🍷 Imported wines from France and Spain — never water
  • 🎂 Elaborate sugar sculptures called "subtleties" displayed between courses
  • 🍖 6–12 courses — a great feast could last 4–5 hours
  • 🥄 Spices were status symbols — the more you used, the richer you were

  • 🍞 Rough brown bread — with grit that gradually wore teeth down to stumps
  • 🥣 Pottage (thick vegetable and grain stew) — eaten almost every day
  • 🧅 Onions, leeks, peas, beans — from the cottage garden
  • 🐟 Salted or dried fish on Fridays — Church rules required it
  • 🍺 Weak ale instead of water — water from rivers and wells was often unsafe
  • 🐭 Pest infestations in grain stores were a constant problem

🗺️ How the Black Death Swept Across Europe

The Black Death did not arrive overnight — it followed the great trade routes of the medieval world westward from Central Asia like an unstoppable tide. By June 1348 it had reached the English port of Weymouth, Dorset, carried by fleas on the rats aboard merchant ships. Within two years, between a third and half of England's entire population was dead. Nothing in human history before or since has killed so many people so quickly.

📅 The Plague's Deadly Journey
1346Black Death reaches the Black Sea — carried along Silk Road trade routes from Central Asia
1347Reaches Sicily, Italy, and southern France — killing up to 50% of some city populations
Jun 1348Arrives at Weymouth, Dorset — via infected merchant ships from the continent
Late 1348Spreads rapidly north through England, reaching London by autumn
1349-50Scotland, Ireland, Scandinavia — virtually nowhere in Europe escapes

🔬 Two Killers in One — Understanding the Disease

Medieval people did not know it, but the Black Death was actually two related diseases striking simultaneously — which is part of why it was so devastating. Even today, Yersinia pestis — the bacterium responsible — is considered one of the most dangerous pathogens ever to affect humans.

🐀 Bubonic Plague
  • 🦟 Carried in the stomach of fleas living on black rats
  • 🔴 Symptoms: painful buboes (swellings the size of an egg) in armpits, groin, and neck
  • ⚫ Black blotches appear as blood pools under the skin
  • 🌡️ Raging fever, vomiting, intense thirst, confusion
  • 💀 Killed 30–70% of those infected — usually within 3–5 days
💨 Pneumonic Plague
  • 💨 Spread through the air — by coughing and sneezing in crowded spaces
  • 🫁 Attacks the lungs directly, causing victims to cough up blood
  • 🤢 Breath becomes foul as lung tissue rots from within
  • 🔵 A bluish skin tint often appears (septicaemic variant)
  • 💀 Almost always fatal — death within just 1–3 days

🩺 Five Theories — All Wrong, But All Logical

Medieval medicine was built on ancient Greek ideas about four "humours" in the body — blood, phlegm, yellow bile, and black bile. Disease meant these were out of balance. Not one of the five main theories about the Black Death was correct — but every single one made perfect logical sense given what people knew at the time. It is unfair to simply mock them.

✝️
God's Punishment
God was punishing humanity for its sins. The cure was prayer, fasting, confession, and sincere repentance. Churches received huge donations as people desperately sought divine protection.
🌿
Miasma — Bad Air
Poisonous vapours rising from rotting matter spread through the air and entered the body. Doctors carried herb-filled pomanders to block the smell. Streets were strewn with sweet-smelling flowers.
⭐
Planetary Alignment
Saturn, Jupiter, and Mars had aligned in 1345, poisoning the atmosphere. Astrologers became essential advisers. Nobody went outside without consulting their horoscope first.
👥
Scapegoating
False accusations that Jewish communities had poisoned water supplies. There was absolutely no evidence. Led to horrific violence across Germany and France.
🩸
Weak Humours
Cold, moist air upset the body's natural balance of humours, making it vulnerable. Doctors prescribed bloodletting (cutting a vein to release excess blood), purging, and avoiding baths.
📜 A 14th-century physician's manual (adapted)
"Flee the disease quickly, go far away, and return slowly. Avoid bathing — it opens the pores to the plague. If a bubo appears, place a live hen against the swelling to draw out the poison. Burn aromatic herbs in every room."

Apply WACA to this source. What does it tell us about medieval medicine — and what did it accidentally get right (isolation/quarantine) alongside what it completely got wrong?

❌ Cures That Didn't Work — But Why People Tried Them

🩸
Bloodletting
Cutting a vein to drain "excess blood" and rebalance the humours. The patient was already weakened by disease — this often made them worse or killed them directly.
🌹
Herb Pomanders
Bunches of herbs and flowers held to the nose to block the "bad air." Actually accidentally helpful — reduced flea exposure. But not enough to prevent infection.
🐔
Live Chicken Remedy
Shaving a chicken's bottom and pressing it against a bubo to "draw out the poison." Completely ineffective — and unpleasant for both patient and chicken.
🙏
Flagellants
Groups of people publicly whipping themselves, believing this would show God sufficient suffering to end the plague. Spread infection as they moved from town to town.
💊
Theriac
An ancient medicine made from vipers' flesh, honey, and 64 other ingredients. Sold as a universal cure. Completely useless against plague.

The Black Death didn't just kill people — it permanently shattered the social structure of medieval England. The consequences rippled outwards for generations, eventually bringing about changes that nobody in 1348 could possibly have predicted.

🌲 Stage 1: The Motte-and-Bailey (1066–1100)

The genius of the motte-and-bailey design was its speed. A skilled team could complete one in as few as 8 days. Within 20 years of the Conquest, over 500 had appeared across England — an extraordinary achievement of military engineering.

🗺️
The MOTTE
A raised mound of earth — sometimes up to 10 metres high. Topped with a wooden tower where the lord lived and kept watch. The height gave defenders a massive tactical advantage: attackers had to climb while being shot at from above.
🏡
The BAILEY
An enclosed courtyard at the base of the motte, surrounded by a wooden palisade fence and a defensive ditch. Housed the stables, kitchens, soldiers' barracks, smithy, and chapel. Life in the bailey was busy and often noisy.
🔥
Critical Weakness: Fire
A single burning arrow on a dry day could destroy years of construction. Wood burns — and Norman England's enemies knew it. One successful fire attack could eliminate an entire castle in hours.
⛏️
Critical Weakness: Rot
Even without attack, English rain rotted the wooden structures within 20–30 years. The Normans needed a permanent solution — and they found it in stone.

🪨 Stage 2: The Stone Keep Castle (1070 onwards)

From around 1070, wealthier barons began replacing wood with stone. The results were magnificent and terrifying — walls 3–4 metres thick that could withstand battering rams, fire, and almost any weapon of the time. These were not just superior defences; they were permanent statements of Norman power, visible for miles across the flat English landscape.

🏰
The Keep
Central stone tower — the lord's home and last refuge. Walls up to 4 metres thick. Contained the great hall, private chambers, and a dark dungeon below ground level.
💧
The Moat
A wide, water-filled ditch surrounding the walls. Impossible to tunnel under without flooding. Made direct assault extremely dangerous.
🚪
The Gatehouse
The most heavily defended point — a drawbridge (that could be raised), a heavy portcullis (iron grille), thick wooden doors, and armed guards. The only way in.
🎯
Battlements
Raised sections of wall with gaps called crenellations. Defenders could step behind the raised parts for shelter, then lean out through the gaps to fire arrows.
🔥
Murder Holes
Openings in the ceiling of the gatehouse passage (called a mantrap). Defenders above could pour boiling water, burning oil, or heavy stones directly onto attackers below.
🌀
Spiral Stairs
Built clockwise when ascending. A right-handed defender coming DOWN the stairs had full sword-swinging room. An attacker going UP had their sword arm blocked by the central pillar. A deliberately lethal design.

🐷 The Siege of Rochester, 1215 — 40 Fat Pigs Win a Battle

Castles were brilliant at defence. But how did attackers actually capture them? In 1215, rebel barons seized Rochester Castle to resist King John. John had to get it back. What followed is one of the most unusual stories in military history.

⚔️ Siege Weapons — The Attacker's Arsenal
  • 🪨 Trebuchet — a counterweight catapult that could hurl 150kg boulders over 300 metres
  • 🪨 Mangonel — a smaller, faster catapult for sustained fire at walls and towers
  • 🔨 Battering ram — a heavy tree trunk swung on chains to smash through gates
  • 🪜 Siege tower — a wooden tower on wheels, rolled up to the walls
  • ⛏️ Mining — tunnels dug beneath corner towers to collapse the foundations
  • ⏳ Siege — simply surround the castle and wait for the defenders to starve
⚔️ The 40 Fat Pigs — Step by Step
Step 1: Siege engines hurl massive boulders at Rochester's walls day and night. The walls hold — they are 3 metres thick at their base.
Step 2: John orders his miners to dig tunnels beneath the south-east tower, weakening its foundations from below.
Step 3: John issues one of history's strangest military orders: "Send to us with all speed, by day and night, forty of the fattest pigs of the sort least good for eating, that we may take them for bringing fire beneath the tower."
Step 4: The pig fat is packed into the tunnel and set alight. It burns at such intense heat — far hotter than wood alone — that the wooden props collapse and the entire corner tower comes crashing down.
Result: Rochester Castle falls. 40 fat pigs achieved what months of conventional siege warfare could not.

📉 Why Did Castles Decline?

For almost 300 years, stone castles were virtually impregnable. But by the 15th century, two developments had made them obsolete almost overnight — and the change was permanent.

💥
Gunpowder Changes Everything
Cannons firing iron cannonballs could batter down stone walls that had previously withstood decades of siege. A wall that could resist a trebuchet for months might fall to a cannon in days. The physics of defence changed permanently.
🕊️
England Becomes More Peaceful
As royal authority strengthened and internal wars decreased, the need for impregnable private fortresses declined. The rich began building comfortable manor houses with large windows instead — comfort over defence.

⛓️ What Was the Transatlantic Slave Trade?

Between 1500 and 1900, approximately 12.5 million Africans were forcibly transported across the Atlantic Ocean to work as enslaved people on plantations in the Americas and Caribbean. It was one of the largest forced migrations in human history — and one of the most profitable enterprises ever organised. Britain became the dominant slave-trading nation by the 18th century, with ships sailing from Bristol, Liverpool and London.

📊 The Triangular Trade
LEG 1 — Britain to Africa: Ships carried goods (guns, cloth, alcohol) to trade for enslaved people.
LEG 2 — The Middle Passage: Enslaved Africans were transported across the Atlantic in horrific conditions. The journey took 6–8 weeks. Up to 1 in 5 died during the crossing.
LEG 3 — Americas to Britain: Ships returned laden with sugar, cotton, tobacco and rum — all produced by enslaved labour.
💰 Who Profited?
  • 🚢 Ship owners and merchants in Bristol, Liverpool, London
  • 🌾 Plantation owners in the Caribbean and Americas
  • 🏦 Banks and insurance companies that funded the trade
  • 🏭 British manufacturers whose goods were traded for people
  • ☕ British consumers who enjoyed cheap sugar, coffee, cotton

🚢 The Middle Passage — A Journey of Suffering

The Middle Passage was the name given to the second leg of the triangular trade — the crossing of the Atlantic. Enslaved people were packed into ships so tightly they could not stand, turn over, or even breathe properly. The Brookes diagram (1787) — showing how 454 people were crammed into a ship designed for 450 — became one of the most powerful pieces of abolitionist propaganda in history.

📜 The Brookes Diagram, 1787 — Visual Source
The diagram showed enslaved people packed into a ship with no room to move, lying on shelves barely 18 inches apart. It was produced by abolitionists to show the British public what the slave trade actually looked like.

🌍 Life Under Slavery

Once enslaved people arrived in the Americas and Caribbean, they were sold at auction — separated from their families and communities forever. On sugar, tobacco, and cotton plantations, they worked from sunrise to sunset under brutal conditions. They had no legal rights, could not marry, and their children were also enslaved from birth.

⏰
Working Conditions
Enslaved people worked 16-18 hour days during harvest. Sugar cane cutting was particularly dangerous — machetes, crushing mills, and boiling sugar caused frequent severe injuries.
⚖️
No Legal Rights
Enslaved people were legally classified as property, not people. They could be sold, beaten, or killed by their owners with minimal legal consequence.
👨‍👩‍👧
Family Separation
Families were routinely separated at auction. Children were sold away from parents. Husbands and wives were split and sent to different plantations miles apart.
✊
Resistance
Despite brutal conditions, enslaved people resisted constantly: working slowly, breaking tools, running away, and organising rebellions. Haiti's revolution (1791) overthrew French colonial rule entirely.
📜 Mary Prince, 'The History of Mary Prince', 1831
"I was flogged to make me work faster. It is not possible to describe the feelings of this horrible punishment. I know what it is to see a person flogged until the blood runs down. The owner of the plantation was very cruel."

Mary Prince was the first enslaved Black woman to publish her autobiography in Britain. Apply WACA: Why is her account particularly significant? What limitations might it have?

⚖️ The Abolition Movement — Fighting Back

The campaign to abolish the slave trade was one of the first great mass protest movements in British history. It used petitions, pamphlets, boycotts of slave-grown sugar, and Parliamentary lobbying over decades. William Wilberforce led the Parliamentary campaign, while Olaudah Equiano and other formerly enslaved people gave the movement a powerful human voice. Women played a crucial role through the Ladies' Anti-Slavery Society and sugar boycotts.

✅ Key Abolition Victories
  • 📅 1772: Somerset Case — enslaved people could not be removed from England against their will
  • 📅 1807: Slave Trade Act — abolished the buying and selling of enslaved people in British ships
  • 📅 1833: Slavery Abolition Act — freed enslaved people across the British Empire
  • 📅 1838: Full emancipation — the "apprenticeship" period ended
❌ The Painful Aftermath
  • 💰 Plantation owners were paid £20 million compensation (£17 billion today) — the enslaved people received nothing
  • ⛓️ Freed people were forced to work as "apprentices" for their former enslavers for 6 more years
  • 🌍 The legacy of slavery — racism, poverty, inequality — continues to affect communities today

🌐 Slavery Today — Modern Slavery

The formal abolition of slavery did not end all forms of forced labour and exploitation. 🏠 The Domestic System — How Things Were Made

In the early 1700s, most manufactured goods were made in people's homes or tiny workshops — the domestic system. A clothier (cloth merchant) bought raw wool from farmers, distributed it to village families who spun it into thread and wove it into cloth, then collected the finished product. Families worked the hours they chose. Children helped. The whole family contributed. This was how Britain had worked for centuries — but it was about to change forever.

✅ Advantages of the Domestic System
  • ⏰ Workers chose their own hours — flexibility for family life
  • 👨‍👩‍👧 Whole family could contribute, including elderly grandparents
  • 🏡 Workers stayed in their homes and communities
  • 🎨 Skilled craft workers took pride in individual work
❌ Disadvantages of the Domestic System
  • 🐌 Slow production — could not meet growing demand
  • 📊 Quality was inconsistent — no standardisation
  • ❄️ No work during winter or illness — no income
  • 💰 Merchants controlled prices and materials

⚙️ The Key Inventions — Why Everything Changed

Between 1733 and 1785, a series of remarkable inventions transformed textile production. Each invention solved a problem created by the previous one, accelerating the pace of change until the domestic system simply could not compete.

🚀
Flying Shuttle (1733)
John Kay's invention allowed weavers to work TWICE as fast — but this created a thread shortage, because spinners couldn't keep up. This problem drove the next invention.
🧵
Spinning Jenny (1764)
James Hargreaves invented a machine that could spin 8 threads at once — later versions spun 80. It was small enough to use at home. But the thread was too weak for warp.
💪
Water Frame (1769)
Richard Arkwright's machine used water power to produce strong, smooth thread. Too large for homes — it needed a factory. This changed EVERYTHING.
🔄
Spinning Mule (1779)
Samuel Crompton combined the Spinning Jenny and Water Frame. Produced strong, fine thread. Required a factory. Weavers now needed power looms to keep up.
🏭
Power Loom (1785)
Edmund Cartwright's steam-powered loom wove cloth mechanically. Now the entire process — spinning AND weaving — could happen in one factory, powered by steam.

🏭 Richard Arkwright and the First Factory

Richard Arkwright, a former barber and wig-maker from Preston, is often called "the father of the factory." His first cotton mill opened at Cromford, Derbyshire in 1771, powered by the River Derwent. It employed 200 workers — including many children — and operated day and night in two shifts. By his death in 1792, Arkwright was worth approximately £500 million in today's money.

👧 Child Labour in the Factories — Child Labour in the Factories

The early factories relied heavily on child labour. Pauper apprentices — orphaned or abandoned children — were sent by workhouses to work in mills, receiving no wages, only food and lodging. Children as young as five worked 14-hour days, operating dangerous machinery in deafening, dusty conditions. The Factory Acts of 1833, 1844, and 1847 gradually improved conditions, but reform came slowly and reluctantly.

😰 Child Workers' Conditions
  • ⏰ 14-16 hour shifts, starting at 5am
  • 🔧 "Scavengers" crawled under moving machinery to collect loose cotton — risking limbs
  • 🌫️ Cotton dust caused permanent lung damage
  • 👊 Beatings for falling asleep or working slowly
  • 😴 Slept in factory dormitories, never seeing daylight
📋 Factory Acts — Reform
  • 📅 1833: No children under 9 in textile factories; under-13s max 9 hours/day; factory inspectors created
  • 📅 1844: Women and children under 13 limited to 12 hours/day
  • 📅 1847: Ten Hours Act — max 10 hours/day for women and young people

🏙️ The Cities That Industrialisation Built — and the Price People Paid

The rapid growth of industrial towns created a public health catastrophe. In 1850, life expectancy in Manchester was just 28 years — lower than in rural areas and lower than it had been a century before. As workers flooded into cities, housing was thrown up as cheaply and quickly as possible. Streets had no drainage. Rivers and wells became contaminated. Disease spread with terrifying speed.

🚰
No Clean Water
Most working-class families got water from shared pumps or directly from rivers — the same rivers used as open sewers. In 1849, a cholera epidemic killed 53,000 people in Britain in a single year.
🪣
Open Sewers
Human waste ran in open channels down the centre of streets. The smell in summer was unbearable. Disease spread through contaminated water and food.
🏚️
Back-to-Back Housing
In Leeds and Manchester, 'back-to-back' houses shared three walls with neighbouring homes. A family of 8-10 might share two small rooms with no ventilation.
🏭
Air Pollution
Factory chimneys filled the air with black smoke 24 hours a day. Respiratory diseases — 'the miner's friend' (tuberculosis) — were rampant. Some cities received barely 2 hours of direct sunlight per day.
🐀
Disease Everywhere
Cholera, typhoid, typhus, and tuberculosis swept through the overcrowded slums. In some areas, infant mortality exceeded 50% — half of all children born died before their fifth birthday.

"I have never seen so ill-built a city as Manchester. The entire south part of the city is covered with such factories. Every main street connects to a series of streets lined with working-class housing. In these streets the air is stagnant, the roads unpaved, mud lies everywhere."

🔬 The Great Investigators — Who Proved What Was Killing People

For decades, most doctors and politicians believed disease was spread by "miasma" — bad air from rotting matter. The breakthrough came when determined reformers began gathering evidence. Their work saved millions of lives.

🗺️ John Snow (1854) — The Broad Street Pump

During the 1854 Soho cholera outbreak, Dr John Snow mapped every death on a street map and traced them all to a single water pump on Broad Street. He removed the pump handle — the outbreak stopped. Snow had proved cholera was spread through contaminated water, not bad air. But the medical establishment refused to believe him for another decade.

📊 Edwin Chadwick (1842) — The Sanitary Report

Edwin Chadwick's Report on the Sanitary Conditions of the Labouring Population (1842) used statistical evidence to prove that poor living conditions caused disease and death — and that cleaning up the cities would save money in the long run. It was one of the first major uses of statistics in social reform.

📜 Edwin Chadwick, 'Report on the Sanitary Conditions of the Labouring Population', 1842
"The annual loss of life from filth and bad ventilation are greater than the loss from death or wounds in any wars in which the country has been engaged in modern times."

🏗️ The Great Reform — Government Cleans Up the Cities

Change came slowly — and only when the problem became impossible to ignore. The Great Stink of 1858 — when the River Thames produced such a terrible smell that Parliament itself was barely able to sit — finally forced action. Engineer Joseph Bazalgette designed London's revolutionary sewer system (1858-1875), which remains largely in use today. The Public Health Acts of 1848 and 1875 forced local councils across Britain to provide clean water, proper sewers, and clear refuse.

📋 Key Reforms
  • 📅 1848: First Public Health Act — allowed (but didn't force) local councils to act
  • 📅 1858: Great Stink — Bazalgette commissioned to build London's sewers
  • 📅 1875: Second Public Health Act — FORCED councils to provide clean water, sewers, refuse collection
  • 📅 1890s: First council housing built in London
📈 The Impact
  • 💀 Cholera never returned to Britain after 1866
  • 📊 Life expectancy in cities rose from 28 to 45 between 1850 and 1900
  • 🏥 Infant mortality fell by 50% between 1870 and 1900
  • 💧 By 1900, most British cities had clean water and proper sewage disposal

🌍 How Did Britain Build the Largest Empire in History?

By 1900, the British Empire covered a quarter of the world's land surface and ruled over 400 million people — more than any empire in history. It included India, large parts of Africa, Australia, Canada, the Caribbean, and dozens of smaller territories. The Empire had been built over centuries through trade, conquest, and colonisation — and it would have profound consequences for millions of people across the globe.

📋 How the Empire Grew
  • ⚓ Trade routes: The East India Company established trading posts in India from 1600
  • ⚔️ Military conquest: Superior weapons and tactics enabled British forces to defeat much larger local armies
  • 🤝 Treaties: Local rulers were often persuaded or pressured to accept British "protection"
  • 🔭 Exploration: British explorers mapped and claimed vast territories in Africa and Asia
  • 🌾 Settlement: British settlers established colonies in Australia, Canada, and South Africa
💰 Why Britain Wanted an Empire
  • 🏭 Raw materials: Cotton from India, rubber from Malaya, gold and diamonds from South Africa
  • 🛒 Markets: 400 million people to sell British manufactured goods to
  • ⚓ Strategic bases: Naval stations allowing Britain to control global trade routes
  • 🏅 National prestige: "The sun never sets on the British Empire"

💎 India — The Jewel in the Crown

India was the most important territory in the British Empire — described by Queen Victoria as "the jewel in the crown." The British East India Company first arrived in 1600 as traders, but gradually expanded its control until it was effectively governing much of India by 1800. ⚓ Britain at War — The Naval Context

While Napoleon's armies swept across Europe, Britain's most powerful weapon was its navy. The strategy was simple: use the Royal Navy to protect merchant ships, seize French colonies, and prevent any invasion fleet from crossing the Channel. The French — obsessed with their revolutionary ideals and land campaigns — largely ignored the naval war. This gave Britain a critical advantage at sea.

⛵ Life at Sea — The Wooden World

A warship like HMS Victory was home to up to 800 men and boys for months at a time. Life was brutal, disciplined, and extraordinarily dangerous — even before any battle began.

  • 🔪 Flogging with a cat-o'-nine-tails for disobedience
  • ⛓️ Impressment: men forcibly seized from streets and ships

  • 🌊 Storms and shipwrecks killed more sailors than enemy action
  • 💀 Disease: typhus, scurvy, dysentery ravaged entire crews
  • 🔥 Fire was catastrophic on a wooden ship full of gunpowder

⚔️ Nelson's Career — From Boy Sailor to National Hero

Horatio Nelson went to sea aged just 12. By his death at 47, he had become Britain's most celebrated commander — inspiring extraordinary loyalty through personal bravery, tactical genius, and genuine care for his men.

1771Born in Norfolk, son of a clergyman. Joined the Royal Navy aged 12.
1793Given command of HMS Agamemnon — his first major independent command.

🧠 The Nelson Touch

What made Nelson truly exceptional was not just his bravery — it was his leadership philosophy. Before Trafalgar, Nelson gathered all his captains together and explained his entire battle plan. This was revolutionary. Other admirals gave orders and expected blind obedience. Nelson gave his captains understanding — and trusted them to use their own judgement in the heat of battle.

🎲

His two-column plan at Trafalgar broke every rule of conventional naval warfare — but it worked.

🌍 Europe in 1914 — A Continent on a Knife Edge

In the summer of 1914, Europe was the most powerful, prosperous, and dangerous place on earth. Decades of rivalry, imperial competition, and military build-up had created a situation where a single spark could ignite a continent-wide war. That spark came on 28 June 1914 — in a street in Sarajevo, Bosnia.

💥 M.A.I.N. — The Four Long-Term Causes

Historians use the acronym M.A.I.N. to describe the four deep, long-term forces that pushed Europe towards war. These causes built up over decades before the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand provided the final trigger.

⚔️
Militarism
European powers — especially Germany and Britain — competed in a massive arms race. Germany built a huge navy; Britain responded by building Dreadnoughts. By 1914, Europe was armed to the teeth and generals were eager to use their new weapons.
🤝
Alliances
Europe had split into two armed camps: the Triple Alliance (Germany, Austria-Hungary, Italy) and the Triple Entente (France, Russia, Britain). These alliances meant a local dispute could drag the whole continent into war.
🌍
Imperialism
European powers competed fiercely for colonies in Africa and Asia. Morocco crises (1905, 1911) brought France and Germany close to war. Imperial rivalry created deep resentments and distrust.
🏴
Nationalism
Intense pride in one's nation drove dangerous rivalries. In the Balkans (the 'powder keg of Europe'), Slavic peoples wanted independence from Austria-Hungary. Serbian nationalism directly triggered the 1914 crisis.

🔫 The Spark — Sarajevo, 28 June 1914

Archduke Franz Ferdinand, heir to the Austro-Hungarian throne, was assassinated in Sarajevo by Gavrilo Princip — a Bosnian Serb nationalist. It was supposed to be a routine royal visit. It became the trigger for the deadliest conflict in human history.

🪖 Life in the Trenches — The Western Front

By Christmas 1914, the German advance had been halted and both sides had dug trenches stretching from the English Channel to Switzerland — over 700km. For four years, millions of men lived and died in these narrow ditches of mud.

😰 The Realities
  • 💧 Mud, water, and rats were constant companions
  • 🦟 Trench foot (flesh rotting in wet boots) affected thousands
  • 💣 Artillery barrages could last days — constant noise and terror
  • ⏱️ Most men spent 90% of their time waiting, not fighting
  • 💀 A single day at the Somme (1 July 1916): 57,470 British casualties

  • 🚜 Tanks: introduced by Britain at the Somme, 1916
  • ✈️ Aeroplanes: reconnaissance first, then dogfights and bombing
  • 💣 Heavy artillery: the defining weapon of WW1
  • 🌊 Submarines: Germany's U-boats threatened to starve Britain

🕊️ The End of the War and Its Legacy

By November 1918, four years of industrial-scale slaughter had exhausted all sides. Germany's allies had collapsed, her army was retreating, and revolution threatened at home. The Armistice was signed at 11am on 11 November 1918. But the peace — embodied in the Treaty of Versailles — would create the conditions for an even deadlier conflict just 21 years later.

🕊️ Why Was the League Created?

The First World War killed 17 million people. When it ended in 1918, world leaders were determined to prevent such a catastrophe from ever happening again. US President Woodrow Wilson proposed a revolutionary idea: an international organisation where countries could settle their disputes peacefully. The League of Nations was born — the world's first attempt at collective security.

🎯 The League's AIMS
  • 🕊️ Prevent war through collective security
  • 🤝 Encourage disarmament among all nations
  • 🌍 Improve living and working conditions globally
  • 📜 Uphold the terms of the Treaty of Versailles
⚠️ Built-In Weaknesses
  • 🇺🇸 USA never joined — Senate refused to ratify
  • ⚔️ No army of its own — could only use economic sanctions
  • 🗳️ Decisions had to be unanimous — one veto blocked everything
  • 🚫 Germany excluded until 1926, USSR excluded entirely

🏛️ How Was the League Organised?

🏛️
The Assembly
All member states met here once a year. One nation, one vote. Decisions had to be unanimous — a single veto could block any action.
📋
The Council
Met more regularly. Britain, France, Italy, Japan were permanent members. 4-11 non-permanent members elected by Assembly.
⚖️
Court of Justice
Based in The Hague, Netherlands. 15 judges settled legal disputes between nations. Had no power to enforce its decisions.
🌍
Commissions
Specialised agencies dealt with global problems: Slavery, Health, Refugees, Labour Conditions. Some achieved remarkable results.

✅ Successes of the 1920s

In the 1920s, when most countries still wanted peace, the League had real successes. But these tended to be smaller disputes between weaker nations — not the great power conflicts it had been designed to prevent.

🏆 Key Successes
1921
Aaland Islands dispute (Finland vs Sweden): League decided in Finland's favour. Both sides accepted. SUCCESS.
1921
Upper Silesia (Germany vs Poland): League divided the territory after a referendum. Both accepted. SUCCESS.
1922
Greek-Turkish refugee crisis: League helped resettle 400,000 refugees. SUCCESS.
1925
Greece vs Bulgaria: Greece invaded; League ordered withdrawal and fined Greece. Both accepted. SUCCESS.
1920s
Commissions: Freed 200,000 slaves in Sierra Leone. Returned 400,000 WW1 prisoners. Fought malaria globally.

❌ Failures of the 1930s — Collapse of Collective Security

The Wall Street Crash of 1929 shattered the fragile peace. Economic desperation brought aggressive nationalist governments to power. The League was tested by major powers — and failed completely.

💔 Key Failures
1923
Corfu Crisis: Italy bombarded Greek island of Corfu. League condemned it but backed down when Italy threatened to leave. FAILURE.
1931
Manchuria: Japan invaded Chinese Manchuria. League took 18 months to investigate. Japan simply left the League. FAILURE.
1935-36
Abyssinia: Italy invaded Abyssinia (Ethiopia). League imposed sanctions — but excluded oil, the one thing that would have worked. Britain and France secretly offered Italy territory. CATASTROPHIC FAILURE.
1936
Remilitarisation: Germany remilitarised the Rhineland. League did nothing. Emboldened Hitler for further aggression. FAILURE.

"It is us today. It will be you tomorrow. Apart from the Kingdom of God, there is not on this earth any nation that is higher than any other."

🔍 Why Did the League Fail?

🇺🇸
USA Absent
The world's most powerful nation never joined. Without US economic and military backing, the League lacked credibility and power.
⚔️
No Army
The League could only impose economic sanctions — and even these required unanimous agreement. It had no teeth.
🗳️
Unanimous Decisions
Any single member could veto action. This made decisive responses to aggression almost impossible.
💰
Economic Depression
After 1929, member states prioritised their own economic recovery over collective action against aggressors.
🏛️
Self-Interest
Britain and France — the League's leaders — often prioritised their own interests over enforcing League rules (see: Hoare-Laval Pact).

📐 Official Edexcel Level Descriptors — 16-Mark Question

Level 4 — 13–16 marks
Analytical explanation directed CONSISTENTLY at the conceptual focus. Coherent, sustained, logically structured line of reasoning. Criteria for judgement justified and applied. NO access if fewer than THREE aspects addressed.
Level 3 — 9–12 marks
Explanation showing SOME ANALYSIS directed at the conceptual focus. Generally sustained reasoning. Judgement given with some justification. Maximum 10 if fewer than THREE aspects addressed.

Explanation showing LIMITED ANALYSIS with implicit links. Some development but reasoning not sustained. Judgement asserted or insecure. Maximum 6 if fewer than THREE aspects addressed.

Simple or generalised answer, lacking development and organisation. Limited knowledge of the period. Judgement missing or asserted without justification.

⚓ Year 8 · Industrial Revolution, 1845
George Weerth: A German Writer Visits Bradford
Source: George Weerth, German writer living in Bradford, written c.1845
“We look in astonishment as our coach drives on. The further we go, the more houses there are, collected along the road. All around we see flames, hissing and rattling. The windows of factories shaking as we go by. The sun darkens as if cloud has blocked it; suddenly it is evening on a bright day! As our horses stop, we see this is a dirty, evil-smelling town.”
WACA Framework
W
Weerth was a German journalist living in England. He was an outsider to British industrial society — giving him a different perspective from those who had grown up with industrialisation.
A
Fellow German intellectuals interested in British capitalism and working conditions.
C
c.1845, at the height of industrialisation. Bradford was one of the fastest-growing and most polluted cities in Britain.
A
To describe and critique the impact of industrialisation on British towns — and implicitly to warn Germany of the same dangers.
Investigation Questions
Q1: Apply WACA. How does Weerth being a German outsider affect the reliability of this source? Is he more or less likely to be objective than a British observer?
Q2: What specific details does Weerth include to convey the negative impact of industrialisation? What has he chosen to describe — and what might he have left out?
Q3: Compare this source with a positive account of industrial Britain. What does the contrast tell us about the limitations of both sources?
Q4: 'Sources written by outsiders are more reliable than those written by insiders.' How far do you agree? Use this source as evidence.

W
Edwin Chadwick was a civil servant and social reformer. He was employed by the government to investigate living conditions — giving him both authority and a potential political motive.
A
Parliament and government ministers — Chadwick was trying to convince politicians to act on public health.
C
1842, before the major Public Health Acts. Cholera had killed tens of thousands in Britain. There was growing pressure for government intervention.
A
To shock politicians into action by using shocking statistics and comparisons with war casualties.

An unnamed British soldier at the Somme. He was an ordinary combatant, giving a ground-level perspective that officers' reports would not include.

October 1916, during the Battle of the Somme — one of the bloodiest battles in history, which killed 57,470 British soldiers on its first day alone.

🌍 Year 9 · League of Nations, 1936

Source: Emperor Haile Selassie of Abyssinia (Ethiopia), addressing the League of Nations in Geneva, June 1936
“I, Haile Selassie I, Emperor of Ethiopia, am here today to claim that justice which is due to my people, and the assistance promised to it eight months ago by fifty-two nations who asserted that an act of aggression had been committed. WACA Framework
W
Haile Selassie was the Emperor of Abyssinia, whose country had just been invaded and conquered by Mussolini's Italy. He had a direct personal and national interest in the outcome of this speech.
A
The 52 member states of the League of Nations — the countries that had promised to protect Abyssinia and had then failed to do so.
C
June 1936. Abyssinia had fallen to Italy. The League's sanctions had failed. This was widely seen as the moment the League died as an effective organisation.
A
To shame the League into action, to make the moral case for collective security, and to ensure the world knew what had happened to his country.

Q3: Why was this speech important as evidence of the League's failure? What does it reveal that official League documents might not?
