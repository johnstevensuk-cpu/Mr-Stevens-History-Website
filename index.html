🎭 Medieval
Entertainment & Life

Leisure was not a right in medieval England — it was a rare gift. The Church structured life around feast days (from which the word 'holiday' derives, via 'holy day'), and only during these precious windows did ordinary people have time to play. What they chose to do reveals as much about the social order as any written chronicle.

🎮 Could You Have Fun in the Middle Ages?

Ordinary people did not have 'holidays'. Instead, there were feast days through the year — Easter, May Day, Midsummer's Eve, Christmas, and various Saints' days. After church, they had some free time. Our word 'holiday' comes from the term 'holy day'. Here are the most popular pastimes:

Bowling / Skittles

✅ Evolved → football
🌰

Became popular after 1066. Drill a cobnut, thread a string — smash opponent's nut.
✅ Still played

✅ Still played

⚽ Football Through the Ages

According to one account, the game began when an English mob kicked around the head of a defeated Danish invader. By the 12th century, mob football was firmly established — and deeply controversial. It was banned more than 30 times between 1314 and 1667.

📋 The (Non-) Rules
  • 👥 Up to 500 players at once
  • 🐷 Ball = pig's bladder stuffed with dried peas
  • 📏 Goals might be several miles apart
  • ⚖️ Any method permitted "except murder"
  • 📅 Usually played on Shrove Tuesday
🚫 The Bans
1314Lord Mayor of London bans it — ignored!
1321Pope pardons player who killed his opponent
1331Edward III — first royal decree banning it
166730+ bans total — still played everywhere

After lunch all the youth of the city go out into the fields to take part in the ball game. The students of each school have their own ball. The workers from each city craft also carry balls. Older citizens, fathers and wealthy men come on horseback to watch.
c.1174 — the first written description of mob football in London on Shrove Tuesday.

🎵 Music, Minstrels & Miracle Plays

The Middle Ages had its own pop songs and dance music. Wandering minstrels were the medieval equivalent of social media — bringing news and entertainment from town to town. Miracle plays drew crowds across 127 different towns.

🎶 Sumer Is Icumen In (13th century)
Summer has come in, Loudly sing, Cuckoo!
The seed grows and the meadow blooms,
And the wood springs anew. Sing, Cuckoo!
The ewe bleats after the lamb,
Merrily sing, Cuckoo!

One of the oldest surviving English songs — passed down without written notation.

👗 Fashion, Status & Sumptuary Laws

👑 Wealthy Nobles
  • ✨ Velvet, silk, fur, fine linen
  • 🎨 Vivid dyes (red=love, yellow=anger, grey=sadness)
  • 👒 Steeple hats — nearly a metre tall in the 1480s
  • 👠 Poulaines — toes so pointed, tied back to ankle
🌾 Ordinary Peasants
  • 🧶 Coarse wool or linen tunics
  • 🟫 Muted colours: grey, brown, dark green
  • 👞 Some owned no shoes at all
  • 🏠 Clothing was handmade and repaired endlessly

Sumptuary Laws were royal decrees forbidding lower classes from wearing fabrics, colours or styles associated with nobility. Their existence is revealing: ordinary people were trying to imitate their social betters — and the ruling class found this threatening enough to require Parliament to stop it.

📖 The English Language After 1066

After the Conquest, English became the third most important language in England — below French and Latin. King Harold was the last monarch whose first language was English. By the 14th century, Geoffrey Chaucer was writing The Canterbury Tales in something very close to modern English.

🔍 What is History?
& The Norman Conquest 1066

History is what historians argue happened, based on evidence they can find and interpret. The year 1066 is the most famous date in English history. Understanding WHY William won — and what changed forever — is where your study of medieval England begins.

📅 Key Date14 October 1066 — Battle of Hastings
🔑 Key ConceptChronology · Provenance · Change
📖 Key MethodWACA — Who, Audience, Context, Aim
🧠 HPL FocusCritical analysis · Curiosity

🧠 The WACA Framework — Apply to Every Source

History is not about memorising dates — it demands asking questions, finding evidence, and building justified arguments. The key skill is WACA: apply it to every source before using it as evidence.

W
WHO made this? Their identity reveals potential bias.
A
Who is the AUDIENCE? Who was it made for?
C
What is the CONTEXT? When? What was happening?
A
What is the AIM? What was the creator trying to achieve?

The judgement: After WACA, write: "This source is useful for understanding [X] because... however it is limited because [WACA finding]..." A biased source is NOT useless — it can be valuable evidence of what the creator wanted people to believe.

👑 Three Men, One Crown — 1066

On 5 January 1066, King Edward the Confessor died without a clear heir. Three men claimed the English throne.

🏴 Harold Godwinson

England's most powerful earl. Claimed Edward named him on his deathbed. Crowned the very next day. Strength: popular, experienced military leader. Weakness: no royal blood.

🛡️ Harald Hardrada

King of Norway. Nicknamed "the Ruthless" — most feared warrior in Europe. Strength: massive Viking army. Weakness: remote claim through an old Viking treaty.

🏰 William of Normandy

Duke of Normandy. Claimed Edward promised him the throne. Harold had sworn an oath to support him. Strength: disciplined army, papal blessing. Nickname: "the Bastard".

⚔️ Two Battles, One Year — Harold's Fatal Mistake

✅ Stamford Bridge (25 September)

Harold rushed north, caught Hardrada's men without armour. Decisive victory. Hardrada killed. Only 24 ships needed to take survivors home. Problem: news arrived that William had landed at Pevensey Bay.

⚠️ Hastings (14 October)

Harold marched 280 miles in 9 days. His exhausted, depleted army faced William's fresh troops at Senlac Hill. The Norman fake retreat drew English soldiers downhill — where they were cut down. Harold killed.

📜 Primary Source A — Account of William at Hastings, written c.1073
Three horses were killed under him. Three times he leapt unafraid to the ground and killed the man who had killed his horse. With savage blows of his sword, he split shields, helmets and coats of chain mail.
Written by William of Poitiers, c.1073 — a Norman writer who did NOT witness the battle. WACA: Who? When? Audience? Aim? Can we fully trust it?

☠️ Health, Medicine
& The Black Death

In the summer of 1348, a catastrophe arrived at the English port of Weymouth. Within two years, between a third and half of England's entire population was dead. Medieval medicine had no answer. Yet from this catastrophe came the slow collapse of the feudal system that had kept peasants in bondage for three centuries.

📅 ArrivedJune 1348 — Weymouth, Dorset
💀 Death Toll~30–50% of England's population
🦠 True CauseYersinia pestis — flea-borne bacteria
📅 Cause IdentifiedNot until 1894 — 546 years later

🖼️ WACA the Danse Macabre (c.1493)

The image above — the Danse Macabre (c.1493) — shows Death taking victims from every social class equally. But apply WACA immediately: it was painted 145 years AFTER the Black Death of 1348. The artist never witnessed the plague. This fundamentally changes how we can use it as evidence.

☠️ Symptoms & What Killed Millions

🦠 Bubonic Plague
  • 🐀 Germ lived in blood of black rats and fleas
  • 🔴 Large buboes (swellings) in armpits and groin
  • ⚫ Red and black spots on arms and thighs
  • 💀 Seven out of ten people would die
💨 Pneumonic Plague
  • 💨 Travelled in the air — caught by breathing
  • 🫁 Attacked the lungs, causing victims to cough blood
  • 🤢 Victims' breath smelled as lungs rotted inside them
  • 💀 Death within a week

❓ What Did People Think Caused It?

📜 Source A — Medieval Italian writer
The plague was a punishment from God.
A genuine belief held by many at the time.
📜 Source D — Doctors at Paris University, 1348
The long term cause is the position of the planets. It is also caused by evil smells which mix with the air and spread on the wind. When you breathe in the corrupted air you catch the plague.
Report by doctors at Paris University — the most educated people of the time.
📜 Source E — Jean de Venette, c.1348
The disease was spread by contagion. If a healthy man visited a plague victim, he usually died himself.
Written c.1348. This is the CLOSEST to the real cause! But why did so few people listen?

⚡ The Causal Chain — Black Death to End of Feudalism

⚡ The Chain of Consequences
1348–50: Black Death kills 30–50% → Labour shortage: survivors could demand higher wages → Lords resisted: Statute of Labourers (1351) tried to cap wages → Peasants ignored it: wages rose anyway → 1381: Peasants' Revolt — thousands marched on London → Long term: Serfdom disappeared. Feudalism collapsed.

🏰 Castles — Motte & Bailey
to Stone Behemoths

After Hastings, William the Conqueror faced a fundamental problem: how do you control a country whose entire population resents you? His answer was stone, timber, mortar and fear. Within months, Norman castles were rising across England — each one a statement to the conquered English: We are here. We are permanent. Try to resist.

⏱️ Build TimeMotte & bailey: as little as 8 days
🏰 ThicknessTower of London: walls up to 2.5m
📊 Scale100+ castles built by 1086

🏗️ Motte-and-Bailey Castles (1066–1100)

By 1086, over 100 castles had been built across England. These had to be built quickly — wherever the Normans needed them. In Cambridge, 25 houses were demolished to make way for a new castle. In Lincoln, the Normans pulled down over 150 houses. The English population had no choice.

🌲 Key Features
  • ⏱️ Built in just 7–14 days
  • 🌍 Motte = raised earthen mound (up to 10m)
  • 🏡 Bailey = enclosed courtyard beside motte
  • 🔒 Wooden fences and walls surrounded it
  • 🗼 Wooden keep on top — last line of defence
⚠️ Weaknesses → Why Go Stone?
  • 🔥 Could be set on fire
  • ⛏️ Could be smashed down
  • 💧 Wood would rot over time
  • 🪜 Walls could be climbed over
  • 💡 So: build in stone from 1070!

🤔 The Big Question: Were Castles Military or Symbolic?

The Tower of London was built next to the River Thames — guarding the key approach to London (military). But it was also massively over-engineered for defence. Its real purpose was to intimidate the English population (symbolic). Apply WACA to the Tower as a SOURCE: Who (William I)? Audience (the conquered English AND his own Norman lords — but with different messages for each)? Context (1078 — 12 years after Hastings)? Aim (military defence AND symbol of permanent Norman power)?

🐷 The Siege of Rochester Castle, 1215 — 40 Fat Pigs!

In 1215, rebel barons seized Rochester Castle to challenge King John. The castle was vital — it guarded the main road to London. John had to get it back.

📜 Primary Source — King John's Order, 1215
Send to us with all speed by day and night, forty of the fattest pigs of the sort least good for eating so that we may bring fire underneath the castle.
King John's written order, 1215. Pig fat burns at up to 500°C — the foundations collapsed and the entire south-east tower fell into the hole.

🏭 Farms to Factories — The Industrial Revolution

📊 Data Analysis📝 Extended Writing💡 Problem Solving💡 IndependenceSDG 1 No PovertySDG 8 Decent WorkSDG 10 Inequalities

Britain transformed from an agricultural society to an industrial powerhouse between 1750–1900. Manchester grew from 25,000 in 1772 to 316,000 by 1851. The Sadler Report of 1833 exposed shocking child labour conditions. This causal chain — Industrialisation → urbanisation → cholera → John Snow → reforms → Beveridge → NHS — is one of history's most important.
