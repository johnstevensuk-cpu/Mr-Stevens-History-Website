🔍 What is History? How Do We Study It?

Before studying any event we must understand HOW historians work. The illustrated panels above show two key primary sources from the Norman Conquest: the Bayeux Tapestry (c.1077) and the Domesday Book (1086). Both are primary sources — but notice the WACA prompts on each panel. Historians apply WACA to EVERY source: Who created it? For what Audience? In what Context? With what Aim? We also work with CHRONOLOGY, CAUSATION and CONSEQUENCE.

Key method: WACA — Who, Audience, Context, AimKey skill: Chronology — BC counts BACKWARDS, AD forwards

⚔️ The Norman Conquest 1066

In 1066 the death of King Edward the Confessor triggered a succession crisis. Three men claimed the throne: Harold Godwinson, Harald Hardrada, and William, Duke of Normandy. Harold defeated Hardrada at Stamford Bridge on 25 September but had to march 250 miles south immediately. At Hastings on 14 October 1066, Harold was killed. ☠️ Health, Medicine & The Black Death

The Danse Macabre panel above — WACA it immediately using the prompt shown: made 145 years AFTER the 1348 Black Death. The Black Death arrived in England in 1348, killing 30-50% of the population. Medieval medicine was completely powerless — physicians used Galen's 1,500-year-old theory of four humours. Long-term: labour shortages gave peasants extraordinary new bargaining power — the feudal system began to collapse.

🏥 Aneurin Bevan, Speech at the formal launch of the National Health Service, 5 July 1948 at Park Hospital, Manchester (now Trafford General)
📍 Source: Aneurin Bevan, Speech at the formal launch of the National Health Service, 5 July 1948 at Park Hospital, Manchester (now Trafford General)
Bevan was the Minister of Health and architect of the NHS. He delivered this speech on the official launch day — the day the first NHS patient was treated. He had overcome fierce BMA opposition to reach this point.
"The NHS will last as long as there are folk left with faith to fight for it. It has not had an easy passage. There have been enormous difficulties. It was my job to overcome these difficulties. We now have the moral leadership of the world."

WACA Question 1
Who gave this speech — and why does Bevan's personal role affect how we use it?
Consider: Bevan was not just a politician giving a speech — he had personally designed and fought for the NHS against significant opposition. How does his role as the NHS's architect affect the source's credibility and purpose?

What claims does Bevan make — and are they justified?
Consider: 'We now have the moral leadership of the world.' Is this claim supported by historical evidence? 'Enormous difficulties' — what were these? (The BMA's opposition is key here.)

How useful is this source for understanding the creation of the NHS?
Consider: This is a celebratory speech on the launch day. Does this make it MORE or LESS useful for understanding what the NHS creation meant? What are its specific limitations?
