🎮 Could You Have Fun?

In the Middle Ages, ordinary people didn't really have holidays. Instead, there were a number of feast days through the year — Easter Day, May Day, Midsummer's Eve, Christmas, and various Saints' days. After church, they would have some free time. Our word 'holiday' comes from the term 'holy day'.

⚽ Football Through the Ages

Over 1000 years ago, a small army from Denmark landed on the English coast. Their leader was captured and the angry English mob chopped off his head. The men split into two groups and kicked the severed head around — and this is how football in Britain began.

📋 Mob Football Facts
  • 👥 Up to 500 players (including women sometimes)
  • ⚖️ Few rules, no referee
  • 📏 Goals were several miles apart
  • 💀 Players could use any method "except murder"
  • 🐷 The ball was a pig's bladder, stuffed with dried peas or sawdust
  • 📅 Usually played on Shrove Tuesday
⏱️ The Football Bans
1314Lord Mayor of London banned it — threat of imprisonment, bans ignored!
1321Pope issued letter forgiving a player who killed an opponent
1331King Edward III — first royal ban by royal decree
1667Football was banned by more than 30 royal or local laws
📜 Source A — William Fitzstephen, c.1174 (first written description of mob football)
"After lunch all the youth of the city go out into the fields to take part in the ball game. The students of each school have their own ball. The workers from each city craft also carry balls. Older citizens, fathers and wealthy men come on horseback to watch the juniors competing, and to revive their own youth."
📜 Source B — A monk's description
"a devilish pastime. More a bloody murdering practice than a sport."

🎵 Let Me Entertain You

The Middle Ages had its own pop songs and dance music. There was no recorded music or pop charts, but listening to a live band — and dancing around — were still very popular in medieval times.

⛪ Sacred Music (Church)

Played only in church. Had no harmonies — just a single singer singing a tune. Not one you'd spend the rest of the day humming!

🏘️ House Music (Outside)

Much livelier! A band of up to a dozen people would play high tempo music. People danced in large circles — clapping, spinning and jumping.

🎵 Organ 🎵 Tambourine 🎵 Flute 🎵 Hurdy Gurdy 🎵 Drum 🎵 Viol 🎵 Clarinet 🎵 Lute 🎵 Cymbal 🎵 Harp 🎵 Trumpet
🎶 Sumer Is Icumen In (13th century)
Summer has come in, Loudly sing, Cuckoo!
The seed grows and the meadow blooms
And the wood springs anew, Sing, Cuckoo!
The ewe bleats after the lamb
The cow lows after the calf.
Merrily sing, Cuckoo!

Source B: A popular song from the thirteenth century — a bit like a pop song of today.

Wandering Minstrels: Singers known as minstrels wandered from town to town entertaining crowds. But minstrels weren't just popular for their singing — often the news they brought from other parts of the country was just as interesting as the show. They knew all the gossip from local towns and villages!

Miracle Plays: Originally put on in church to teach peasants about the Bible or about saints. Over time, they became morality tales performed by groups of travelling actors who toured the country. Records show that plays were performed in 127 different towns during the Middle Ages.

👗 Keeping in Fashion

Fashion was important in the Middle Ages too. The rich dressed in fancy clothes to impress each other. Clothes became a status symbol, with lords and ladies trying to outdo each other.

👑 What the Rich Wore

Fine wool, leather, linen, velvet, silk, or fur. Decorated with gold and silver thread, jewels, chains, and fancy buttons.

❤️ Red = In love 💛 Yellow = Anger 🩶 Grey = Sadness
🌾 What the Poor Wore
  • 🧶 Plain tunics in coarse wool or linen
  • 🟫 Dull colours: grey, brown, dark green
  • 👞 Some couldn't even afford shoes
  • 🏠 Clothes nearly always handmade — would last for years

Hats and shoes: Women wore fancy hats shaped like animal horns or butterfly wings. The steeple hat, fashionable in the 1480s, was nearly a metre tall! Pointed shoes called poulaines were so fashionable that the toes had to be tied back. Platform shoes called pattens were common for walking through muddy streets.

📖 The Story of the English Language

Did you know that for many years after 1066, English was the third most important language in England — below French (spoken by nobles) and Latin (used by the Church)? So how did the English language survive?

⚔️ Anglo-Saxon

Words from Angles, Saxons and Jutes still survive today:

drink · father · ground · love · name · son · the · wife · was
⛪ Latin

When Britain became Christian, Latin words came into use:

bishop · church · monk · alter ego · factor · parent
🛡️ Norse (Viking)

From the 700s, Vikings added words. Many in place names ending in 'by':

anger · build · gate · glitter · oaf · neck · skin · ugly · you

In 1066, William the Conqueror brought the French language to England. King Harold was the last Old English-speaking king! French words still used today: archer, army, arrest, baron, battle, castle, city, enemy, grape, guard, judge, market, plate, prison, sausage, sugar, traitor.

Old English didn't die because ordinary peasants spoke it every day — they wouldn't and couldn't learn French or Latin. As centuries passed, Old English and French began to mix. By the 1400s, Chaucer was writing the Canterbury Tales in English — often called the 'saviour of the English language'. Edward III allowed English to be used in Parliament; Henry IV became the first monarch in over 300 years to make his first speech as a king in English.

Peasants grew strong-flavoured vegetables and herbs: onions, garlic, leeks, cabbage, carrots, parsley, mint, apples, cherries, pears, wild berries, honey. The most common meat was bacon — pigs were easy to keep. Unusual meats included squirrel, hedgehog, pike, eel. No refrigerators meant meat was salted or smoked to keep it from going bad.

🥔 Key Fact Potatoes didn't reach England until the 1500s — no chips for medieval people! And a peasant's diet was probably much healthier than most people's today — no large amounts of sugar, and they worked all day so they got lots of exercise.

☠️ Health, Medicine
& The Black Death

The Black Death arrived in England in 1348, killing 30–50% of the population within two years. Medieval medicine was completely powerless. Long-term: labour shortages gave surviving peasants extraordinary new bargaining power — and the feudal system began to collapse.

🏭 Farms to Factories — The Industrial Revolution

📊 Data Analysis📝 Extended Writing💡 Problem SolvingSDG 8 Decent WorkSDG 10 Inequalities

Britain transformed from an agricultural society to an industrial powerhouse between 1750–1900. Manchester grew from 25,000 in 1772 to 316,000 by 1851. The Sadler Report of 1833 exposed shocking child labour conditions. This Industrial Revolution → urbanisation → cholera → reforms → Beveridge → NHS chain is one of history's most important causal chains.

🏙️ Victorian Living Conditions & Public Health

📊 Data Analysis🔬 Research Skills🔗 ConnectionsSDG 3 Good Health

Victorian cities grew explosively but without planning. Dr John Snow's mapping of the 1854 Soho cholera outbreak proved water caused cholera — a landmark in scientific detective work. The Great Stink of 1858, when the Thames became so polluted it drove MPs out of Parliament, finally forced investment in Bazalgette's sewage system.

🔍 Activity 1: John Snow's Map Investigation
  • How did John Snow PROVE cholera was waterborne? What was his methodology?
  • Why did the Great Stink FINALLY force political action when cholera deaths alone had not?
📢 Activity 2: Public Health Campaign Poster
  • Design a Victorian poster persuading the government to invest in clean water and sewers.
  • Use specific evidence: cholera death rates, John Snow's findings, the Great Stink.

🕊️ The League of Nations — Ideals & Failures

Created after WW1 to prevent future wars, the League had genuine early successes but fatal structural weaknesses. The USA never joined — the Senate refused by 49 votes to 35. No army.
