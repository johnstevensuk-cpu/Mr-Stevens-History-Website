What is History? How Do We Study It?

History is not about memorising dates — it is about asking questions, weighing evidence, and building justified arguments. Before studying any event we must understand HOW historians work. The Bayeux Tapestry (c.1077) is a perfect starting point: apply WACA immediately. The Domesday Book (1086) is another — both are shown above.

🔍 Activity 1: WACA the Bayeux Tapestry
  • W — Who? Bishop Odo of Bayeux — William's half-brother commissioned it, probably made by Anglo-Saxon embroiderers
  • A — Audience? Norman lords and Church officials
  • C — Context? 1070s, within 10 years of the Conquest
  • A — Aim? Celebrate Norman victory and legitimise William's rule
  • Key question: Does knowing Bishop Odo commissioned it make it more or less reliable as evidence of Hastings?
📅 Activity 2: Chronology Challenge
  • BC counts BACKWARDS — 100 BC is 500 years BEFORE 400 BC
  • Put in order: AD 1066, 350 BC, AD 1348, 55 BC, AD 1807
  • HOMEWORK: Create a personal timeline of YOUR life and major world events
⚖️ Activity 3: Primary vs Secondary Sources
  • PRIMARY = created AT THE TIME. SECONDARY = created AFTER using primary sources
  • Classify: Bayeux Tapestry (c.1077) / 2024 BBC documentary / WW1 soldier's letter / your history textbook
  • Extension: Can a primary source ever be LESS reliable than a secondary one? Give an example.
🔗 Activity 4: Cause and Effect
  • CAUSE = WHY something happened. EFFECT = WHAT happened as a result
  • Example chain: William won Hastings → England got a new king → Castles built across England → Anglo-Saxons lost their land
  • Create your own chain of 3+ links from this year's topics

The Norman Conquest 1066

In 1066 the death of King Edward the Confessor triggered a succession crisis. Three men claimed the throne: Harold Godwinson, Harald Hardrada, and William of Normandy. Harold defeated Hardrada at Stamford Bridge on 25 September — but had to march 250 miles south immediately. At Hastings on 14 October 1066, Harold was killed.

⚔️ Activity 1: Three Claimants Investigation
  • Create a detailed fact-file for Harold Godwinson, Harald Hardrada AND William of Normandy
  • For each: their claim, main strength, main weakness, most likely to win?
  • Who had the strongest LEGAL claim? Who had the strongest MILITARY claim?
📜 Activity 2: Source Analysis
  • Apply full WACA to the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle (c.1100 — written 34 years after the events):
  • W: monks — educated, Anglo-Saxon. For whom? In what context? With what aim?
  • Compare with the Bayeux Tapestry: what can each tell us that the other CANNOT?

Medieval Entertainment & Life

Holy days gave peasants rare free time for games, sport and feasting. The Church dominated every aspect of life. Mob football was banned by Edward III in 1363. Health, Medicine & The Black Death

The Black Death arrived in England in 1348, killing 30–50% of the population within two years. Medieval medicine was completely powerless. The Danse Macabre (c.1493) — apply WACA immediately: it was made 145 years AFTER the Black Death. Long-term: labour shortages gave peasants extraordinary new bargaining power.

☠️ Activity 1: Royal Physician's Report
  • You are Edward III's physician in 1349. Write your official report:
  • Section 1 — Symptoms (at least THREE)
  • Section 2 — The THREE main medieval theories for the cause (God, miasma, planets)
  • Section 3 — Available treatments and an honest assessment of whether any work
🔗 Activity 2: Consequence Chains
  • Draw 3 consequence chains showing how the Black Death led to:
  • The Peasants' Revolt of 1381 (labour shortages → higher wages → peasants' demands)
  • Changes in the Church's authority
  • Changes in medicine and public health
🔬 Research Project

  • 🥈 Silver: Explain HOW the Black Death spread. Why were towns more affected? Include trade routes, rats, fleas and sanitation.
  • 🥇 Gold: Compare ALL FOUR medieval explanations. For each: WHY did people believe it? WHY was it wrong?
  • 💎 Platinum: Evaluate — 'The Black Death was the most important event in medieval English history.' Use all resources.

Slavery & the Slave Trade

The transatlantic slave trade forcibly transported over 12 million Africans to the Americas over 400 years. The Brookes diagram (1787) was produced by ABOLITIONISTS — knowing this fundamentally changes how we use it as evidence. Britain abolished the slave TRADE in 1807 and slavery itself in 1833.

📜 Activity 1: WACA the Brookes Diagram
  • Apply full WACA: Who produced it? For what Audience? In what Context? With what Aim?
  • Key question: The Brookes was made by abolitionists. Does knowing their aim make it more or less useful?
  • Compare with Equiano's written account — what can each tell us that the other cannot?
✍️ Activity 2: Abolition Campaign Design
  • Research Wilberforce, Equiano, Clarkson and the Quakers
  • Why did it take until 1807 to abolish the slave TRADE, and another 26 years until 1833 for slavery itself?

Farms to Factories — The Industrial Revolution

Britain transformed from an agricultural society to an industrial powerhouse between 1750–1900. Manchester grew from 25,000 in 1772 to 316,000 by 1851. The Sadler Report of 1833 exposed shocking child labour conditions. This Industrial Revolution → urbanisation → cholera → reforms → Beveridge → NHS chain is one of history's most important causal chains.

🏭 Activity 1: Factory Inspector's Report

  • Include: hours worked by children, safety hazards, wages, and your recommendations for reform.
🔗 Activity 2: Causal Chain
  • Create a detailed causal chain: Industrialisation → urbanisation → cholera → John Snow (1854) → reforms → Beveridge Report (1942) → NHS (1948)
  • For each link: write 2–3 sentences explaining WHY it led to the next step

Victorian Living Conditions & Public Health

Victorian cities grew explosively but without planning. Dr John Snow's mapping of the 1854 Soho cholera outbreak proved water caused cholera — a landmark in scientific detective work. The Great Stink of 1858, when the Thames became so polluted it drove MPs out of Parliament, finally forced investment in Bazalgette's revolutionary sewage system.

🔍 Activity 1: John Snow's Map Investigation
  • Plot cholera deaths on a street map of Soho 1854. Identify the pattern and the contaminated pump on Broad Street.
  • How did John Snow PROVE cholera was waterborne? What was his methodology?
  • Why did the Great Stink of 1858 FINALLY force political action when cholera deaths alone had not?
📢 Activity 2: Public Health Campaign Poster
  • Design a Victorian poster persuading the government to invest in clean water and sewers.
  • Use specific evidence: cholera death rates, John Snow's findings, the Great Stink.

Admiral Horatio Nelson became Britain's greatest naval hero at the Battle of Trafalgar (21 October 1805). His unconventional two-column attack broke the Franco-Spanish formation.
