What is History? How Do We Study It?

🔍 What is History? How Do We Study It?

Before studying any event, we must understand HOW historians work. History is the process of asking questions about the past, finding evidence, evaluating it critically, and constructing justified arguments. The Bayeux Tapestry (above left, c.1077) is a perfect primary source — but who made it, for whom, and with what purpose? The Domesday Book (above right, 1086) is another: William's comprehensive land survey. Historians always apply WACA. We also work with CHRONOLOGY (ordering events in time), CAUSATION (why things happened) and CONSEQUENCE (what resulted). PRIMARY sources were created at the time; SECONDARY sources were created later.

Key method: WACA — Who, Audience, Context, AimKey skill: Chronology — BC counts BACKWARDS, AD forwardsKey concept: Primary vs Secondary sources

The Norman Conquest 1066

⚔️ The Norman Conquest 1066

In 1066, the death of King Edward the Confessor without an heir triggered a succession crisis. Three men claimed the throne: Harold Godwinson, Harald Hardrada of Norway, and William, Duke of Normandy. Harold defeated Hardrada at Stamford Bridge on 25 September — but had to march 250 miles south immediately. At Hastings on 14 October 1066, Harold was killed. The Bayeux Tapestry (above left) records these events — apply WACA to it! The Domesday Book (above right, 1086) shows how William consolidated control through a comprehensive land survey — an administrative revolution.

🎭 Medieval Entertainment & Life

Health, Medicine & The Black Death

☠️ Health, Medicine & The Black Death

The 'Danse Macabre' by Wolgemut (above, c.1493) shows Death taking victims from every social class. The Black Death arrived in England in 1348, killing 30–50% of the population within two years. Medieval medicine was completely powerless. Physicians used Galen's 1,500-year-old theory of four humours. The three main explanations were: God's punishment, bad air (miasma theory), or planetary alignment. Long-term: labour shortages gave peasants extraordinary bargaining power — the feudal system began to collapse.

Castles — Motte & Bailey to Stone Behemoths

🏰 Castles — Motte & Bailey to Stone Behemoths

The Tower of London (above) — begun by William around 1078 — is the most powerful surviving symbol of Norman conquest, with walls 3–4 metres thick. After 1066, William built motte-and-bailey castles at extraordinary speed: a wooden tower on a raised earthen mound (motte), surrounded by an enclosed yard (bailey), buildable in days. Over the following century these evolved into stone keeps. Castles served as military strongholds, symbols of Norman power, administrative centres, and residences for the lord's household.

Industrial Revolution — Farms to Factories

🏭 Industrial Revolution — Farms to Factories

Loutherbourg's 'Coalbrookdale by Night' (above, 1801) captures the Industrial Revolution's transformation: furnaces blazing, smoke filling the air. Between 1750 and 1900, Britain became the world's first industrialised nation. Manchester grew from 25,000 in 1772 to over 300,000 by 1850. The Sadler Report of 1833 exposed shocking child labour conditions. This chain — Industrial Revolution → urbanisation → cholera → Beveridge Report (1942) → NHS (1948) — is one of history's most important long-term consequences.

1769 Watt's steam engine1833 Sadler Report1948 NHS created

Victorian Living Conditions

🏙️ Victorian Living Conditions & Public Health

The rapid growth of industrial cities created catastrophic public health crises. Overcrowded housing, open sewers and contaminated water created perfect conditions for cholera. Dr John Snow's mapping of the 1854 Soho cholera outbreak — tracing all deaths to a contaminated pump on Broad Street — proved water (not miasma) caused cholera: a landmark in public health history. The Great Stink of 1858 finally forced investment in Bazalgette's revolutionary sewage system.

1832,48,54 Major cholera outbreaks1854 John Snow maps cholera

The 'Imperial Federation' map of 1886 (above) shows British territories in red — one quarter of the world's land surface, governing 400 million people. Apply WACA to this map! Nelson & The Battle of Trafalgar 1805

⛵ Nelson & The Battle of Trafalgar 1805

Stanfield's 1836 painting (above) captures HMS Victory at the centre of the engagement — it was painted 31 years AFTER the battle, which matters for WACA. On 21 October 1805, Nelson commanded 27 British ships against 33 Franco-Spanish vessels. His unconventional two-column attack broke the enemy formation and allowed British ships to fight at close quarters, where their superior gunnery was decisive. Britain won without losing a single ship. Nelson died in battle at 4:30pm. The victory gave Britain unchallenged naval supremacy for over a century.

21 Oct 1805 Battle of TrafalgarResult: 22 enemy ships taken, none British lostWACA note: Stanfield painting made 31 years after the battle

🪖 World War One — Causes, Trenches & Technology

These two photographs tell WW1's story. Left: Cheshire Regiment soldiers in a Somme trench, July 1916 — an official IWM photograph. Right: British troops going 'over the top'. WW1 grew from M.A.I.N. causes: Militarism (arms race), Alliances (two armed blocs), Imperialism (colonial rivalry) and Nationalism (rising tensions). The assassination of Franz Ferdinand at Sarajevo on 28 June 1914 was the spark — not the underlying cause. The Somme (July–November 1916) saw 60,000 British casualties on day one.

The League of Nations — Ideals & Failures

🕊️ The League of Nations — Ideals & Failures

The photograph above shows Emperor Haile Selassie of Ethiopia addressing the League of Nations Assembly in Geneva, June 1936, after Italy's invasion of Abyssinia. His question — 'What have you done for Ethiopia?' — exposed the League's fundamental failure. The League was founded in 1920 on Wilson's 14 Points. When Japan invaded Manchuria (1931) and Italy invaded Abyssinia (1935–36), the League proved completely powerless.
