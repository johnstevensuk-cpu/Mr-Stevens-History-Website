Medieval
Entertainment
& Life

In the Middle Ages, ordinary people didn't really have holidays. Instead, there were a number of feast days through the year — Easter Day, May Day, Midsummer's Eve, Christmas, and various Saints' days. After church, they would have some free time. In fact, our word 'holiday' comes from the term 'holy day'.

5.4

Could you have fun in the Middle Ages?

People generally made their own fun, creating home-made equipment with whatever they had to hand. Some of the sports and games were so popular that they are still enjoyed today.

🎮 Games & Pastimes — Who Could Play?
🎳
Bowling
Players would take it in turns to knock down as many skittles as they could with three balls.
✅ Still played today
⚽
Mob Football
No rules, no referee and as many players as you can get. Whole villages would play each other, with the goals several miles apart.
✅ Still played today
🌰
Conkers
Became popular after 1066. Find a cobnut, drill a hole, thread onto a string. Taking turns, try to smash your opponent's cobnut to bits.
✅ Still played today
⛸️
Ice Skating
People would strap sharpened bones to their feet to use as skates. Archaeologists have found a young boy's skeleton with skates still strapped on his feet. Thin ice perhaps?
✅ Still played today
⛳
Golf
Using a few basic clubs, players would hit a leather ball stuffed with hair. Popular in Scotland and Holland by 1500.
✅ Still played today
🙈
Cold Hand
A player would be blindfolded in front of a crowd and slapped by one of them. He or she would have to guess who hit them. Also called 'blind man's buff' or 'hot cockles'.
✅ Blind man's buff still exists
🐓
Cock Fighting
Two birds were forced to attack each other, sometimes with metal tied to their claws. People would bet on the result.
❌ Now illegal — animal cruelty
🦵
Shin Hacking
Two villagers would kick each other in the shins as hard as they could until one of them couldn't take the pain and gave up.
❌ No longer practised
🪑
Stoolball
A young lady would sit on a stool and men would throw a ball at her. She would try to dodge, perhaps using a bat to hit it away. If they hit her — they got a kiss!
✅ Ancestor of cricket
🐻
Bear Baiting
A bear would be chained to a post while dogs attacked it. People would bet on the result — would the dogs or the bear win?
❌ Now illegal — animal cruelty
🤼
Wrestling
People loved all sorts of fighting games — the more blood the better.
✅ Still a sport today
🏹
Archery
England's armies always needed archers. Boys had to practise from a very young age.
✅ Still an Olympic sport
👑 What about the Rich?
🏰 Rich Nobles

Exclusive Entertainment

  • 🗡️ Tournaments — sword fighting, jousting, horse-riding with prizes for the best competitors
  • 🦌 Hunting on horseback across their estates
  • 🍽️ Elaborate feasting and dancing in the great hall
  • 🤹 Acrobats and jugglers performing for guests
  • ♟️ Chess, draughts, cards, dice games
  • 🎾 Real tennis — hit a wooden ball over a rope; balls hit so hard they could kill on impact
🌾 Ordinary Peasants

Make Your Own Fun

  • 🎉 Holy days (feast days) were rare and precious — they made sure to enjoy every minute
  • 🎮 Home-made equipment using whatever was to hand
  • ⛪ Every holy day began with a church service
  • 🏘️ Village-wide games like mob football — whole villages playing each other
  • 🎭 No cinemas, no theatres, no organised professional sport
  • 🤲 "Do it yourself" — whether rich or poor, you had to make your own fun

5.5

Has football changed much since the Middle Ages?

Over 1000 years ago, a small army from Denmark landed on the English coast. Their leader was captured and the angry English mob chopped off his head. The men split into two groups and kicked the severed head around — and this is how football in Britain began.

⚽ Mob Football — The Facts
📋 The Rules (what rules?)
  • 👥 Up to 500 players (including women sometimes)
  • ⚖️ Few rules, no referee
  • 📏 Goals were several miles apart
  • 💀 Players could use any method "except murder"
  • 🐷 The ball was a pig's bladder, stuffed with dried peas or sawdust
  • 📅 Usually played on Shrove Tuesday (Pancake Day)
  • 🎊 Sometimes played with several balls at once
💥 The Consequences
  • 🩸 Players would "retire home as from battle"
  • 🦴 Bloody heads, bones broken and out of joint
  • ☠️ In 1321 the Pope issued a special letter of forgiveness to a player who had accidentally killed an opponent
  • 🚫 By 1450 a new rule in some towns: the ball could only be kicked, not carried or thrown
📜 Primary Sources
SOURCE A — William Fitzstephen, 1174 (first description of mob football)
"After lunch all the youth of the city go out into the fields to take part in the ball game. The students of each school have their own ball. The workers from each city craft also carry balls. Older citizens, fathers and wealthy men come on horseback to watch the juniors competing, and to revive their own youth. You can see their inner passions aroused as they watch the action and get caught up in the fun."
— Description of London, c.1174. The first written description of mob football played in London on Shrove Tuesday.
SOURCE B — A monk's description of mob football
"a devilish pastime. More a bloody murdering practice than a sport."
— A more modern view of football in the Middle Ages.
🚫 The Football Bans — A Timeline
1314
Lord Mayor of London banned football: "The hustling over large footballs causes great uproar in the city." Despite the threat of imprisonment — the ban was ignored!
1321
The Pope issued a special letter of forgiveness to a player who had accidentally killed an opponent.
1331
King Edward III became the first king to officially ban football by royal decree. He said people were forgetting to practise their archery skills — if a foreign army invaded, men would have forgotten how to use their bows and arrows.
1450
Players in some towns introduced a new rule to reduce accidents: the ball could only be kicked, not carried or thrown.
1314–1667
Football was officially banned by more than 30 royal or local laws. Richard II, Henry IV and Henry V all tried to ban it — but people carried on regardless. In Scotland, King James I famously ruled that "na man play at the fute-ball" — but the Scots were playing in Edinburgh the very next week.

5.6

Let me entertain you

The Middle Ages had its own pop songs and dance music. There was no recorded music or pop charts, but listening to a live band — and dancing around — were still very, very popular in medieval times.

🎵 Music and Dance
⛪ Sacred Music (Church)

Played only in church. Had no harmonies — just a single singer singing a tune. Not one you'd spend the rest of the day humming!

🏘️ House Music (Outside Church)

Much livelier! A band of up to a dozen people would play high tempo music. People danced in large circles — clapping, spinning and jumping. Singing over a mug of ale on the village green was very popular.

🎶 A Medieval Pop Song — "Sumer Is Icumen In" (13th century)
Sumer Is Icumen In
Summer has come in, Loudly sing, Cuckoo!
The seed grows and the meadow blooms
And the wood springs anew, Sing, Cuckoo!
The ewe bleats after the lamb
The cow lows after the calf.
The bullock stirs, the stag farts,
Merrily sing, Cuckoo!

Source B: A popular song from the thirteenth century — a bit like a pop song of today, often sung in summer when the crops were growing well.

🎪 Wandering Minstrels

Singers known as minstrels wandered from town to town entertaining crowds. They sometimes sang for the lord in his castle, but generally played on the streets so people could dance and sing along — expecting a few coins in return.


But minstrels weren't just popular for their singing. Often the news they brought from other parts of the country was just as interesting as the show. Wandering around so much meant they knew all the gossip from local towns and villages!

🎭 Miracle Plays

Plays were another popular form of live entertainment and drew large crowds. Originally put on in church to teach peasants about the Bible or about saints. Over time, they became morality tales where good overcame evil — so popular they were moved outside.


They were called miracle plays and were usually performed by groups of travelling actors who toured the country. Records show that plays were performed in 127 different towns during the Middle Ages — enjoyed by thousands.

Source C: Miracle plays were common in midland and northern towns such as Wakefield and Chester.

5.7

Keeping in fashion

Fashion was important in the Middle Ages too. The rich dressed in fancy clothes to impress each other. Clothes became a status symbol, with lords and ladies trying to outdo each other.

👗 Dress to Impress — What the Rich Wore

The rich wore the latest costumes made from fine wool, leather, linen, velvet, silk, or fur. They decorated these clothes with gold and silver thread, jewels, chains, and fancy buttons.

🎨 The Meaning of Colours
❤️ Red = You are in love
💛 Yellow = Anger
🩶 Grey = Sadness

Natural dyes — made from plants, berries, nuts, crushed insects, and even rust — were used to make the different coloured cloth.

🎩 Hats, Shoes & How Fashion Changed

Hats: Women often hid their hair beneath fancy hats — some shaped like animal horns or butterfly wings. The famous 'steeple hat', fashionable in the late 1480s, was nearly a metre tall!

Shoes: Pointed shoes called 'poulaines' were very fashionable. Some were so pointed that the toes had to be tied back. Platform shoes called 'pattens' were common too, for walking through the filthy, muddy streets.

Change over time: As the Middle Ages progressed — dresses got longer, hats got taller, cloth got brighter, and shoes got pointier. Just like today, fashions varied from year to year.

👘 Did Peasants Follow Fashion?
👑 Rich
  • ✨ Velvet, silk, fur, linen, fine wool
  • 💎 Gold/silver thread, jewels, chains
  • 🎨 Brightly dyed fabrics in meaningful colours
  • 👒 Elaborate, changing hats every decade
  • 👠 Pointed poulaines — sometimes tied back at the toe
🌾 Peasants
  • 🧶 Plain tunics in coarse wool or linen
  • 🟫 Dull colours: grey, brown, dark green
  • 🧣 Woollen leggings, straw or woollen hats, hoods, capes
  • 👞 Some couldn't even afford shoes
  • 🏠 Clothes nearly always handmade — would last for years

5.8

The story of the English language

Did you know that for many years after 1066, English was the third most important language in England? So how did the English language survive?

📖 Before 1066 — The Roots of Old English
⚔️ Anglo-Saxon

Angles, Saxons and Jutes from Germany and Denmark invaded c.1,500 years ago. Their words still survive today.

drink · father · ground · love · name · silly · son · the · was · wife
⛪ Latin

When Britain became Christian, Latin words (mainly connected with the Church) came into use.

bishop · church · monk · alter ego · factor · parent · etc.
🛡️ Norse (Viking)

From the 700s, Vikings added their words. Many connected to place names: 'by' (settlement) = Grimsby, Whitby.

anger · build · dregs · gate · glitter · oaf · neck · skin · ugly · you
🇫🇷 After 1066 — French Takes Over

In 1066, William the Conqueror brought the French language to England. King Harold was the last Old English-speaking king!

All important people spoke French — the queen, barons, their wives and children. They brought new words still used today:

archerarmyarrestbaronbattlecastlecityenemygrapeguardjudgemarketplateprisonsausagesugartraitor

After 1066, Old English was officially the third most important language in England. Nobody wrote it down any more. Kings spoke French and Latin; ordinary peasants spoke Old English.

🤝 English Survives — and Grows

Old English didn't die because ordinary peasants and townspeople spoke it every day. They wouldn't and couldn't learn French or Latin.

As centuries passed, Old English and French began to mix. By the 1400s, something very similar to modern English had emerged.

SOURCE B — From a modern history book about Chaucer
"Chaucer has often been referred to as the 'saviour of the English language'. As a writer of the famous Canterbury Tales, he wrote mainly in English — the language of ordinary people — but used French words when he didn't think an English one fitted. For example, instead of using the word 'hard' (Old English) he may choose the word 'difficult' (French). He thought his writing was improved by the richness of the English language. He had so many words to choose from — Old English, French, Latin, Norse and Anglo-Saxon."
📌 Key Fact Many medieval kings spoke French, and couldn't read or write either! It was Edward III who realised that English, not French or Latin, was still spoken by millions of ordinary people — and allowed English to be used in Parliament and in law courts. Soon after, Henry IV became the first monarch in over 300 years to make his first speech as a king in English.

Source A: A peasant's daily meals. There was no tea, coffee, orange juice or fizzy drinks — and the water wasn't usually fit to drink, so they drank a watery kind of beer called ale.

🥕 A Surprisingly Healthy Diet
🌿 Vegetables & Fruit

Peasants grew strong-flavoured vegetables and herbs to make their dull food tastier:

onions garlic leeks cabbage carrots parsley mint apples cherries pears wild berries honey
🥩 Meat — When They Could Get It

The most common meat was bacon — pigs were easy to keep.

Unusual meats people today would find surprising: squirrel, hedgehog, pike, eel.

No refrigerators → meat was salted or smoked to keep it from going bad. Strips were hung from cottage roofs to dry out.

🥔 Key Fact Potatoes didn't reach England until the 1500s — so there were no chips or potato wedges for the peasants to enjoy! And a peasant's diet was probably much healthier than most people's today — they didn't eat food containing large amounts of sugar, and they worked all day so they got lots of exercise.
