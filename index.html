🎭 Medieval
Entertainment & Life

Leisure was not a right in medieval England — it was a rare gift. The Church structured life around feast days (from which our word 'holiday' derives, via 'holy day'), and only during these precious windows did ordinary people have time to play.

🎮 Could You Have Fun?

Ordinary people didn't really have 'holidays'. Instead, there were feast days through the year — Easter, May Day, Midsummer's Eve, Christmas and Saints' days. After church, they would have some free time. In fact, our word 'holiday' comes from the term 'holy day'.

⚽ Football Through the Ages

According to legend, the game began when an English mob kicked around the severed head of a defeated Danish invader. By the 12th century, mob football was firmly established — and deeply controversial. It was banned more than 30 times between 1314 and 1667.

📜 Source A — William Fitzstephen, c.1174 (first written description of mob football)
“After lunch all the youth of the city go out into the fields to take part in the ball game. The students of each school have their own ball. The workers from each city craft also carry balls.”
Written c.1174 — the first written description of mob football in London on Shrove Tuesday.
📜 Source B — A monk's description
“a devilish pastime. More a bloody murdering practice than a sport.”
🚫 The Football Bans
1314 — Lord Mayor of London banned it. Threat of imprisonment — completely ignored!  |  1321 — Pope issued letter forgiving player who killed opponent.  |  1331 — Edward III banned it by royal decree.  |  1667 — 30+ bans in total. Still played.

🎵 Music, Minstrels & Miracle Plays

The Middle Ages had its own pop songs and dance music. Wandering minstrels were the medieval equivalent of social media — bringing news and entertainment from town to town. Miracle plays drew crowds of thousands across 127 different towns.

🎶 Sumer Is Icumen In (13th century)
Summer has come in, Loudly sing, Cuckoo!
The seed grows and the meadow blooms,
And the wood springs anew. Sing, Cuckoo!
The ewe bleats after the lamb,
Merrily sing, Cuckoo!

One of the oldest surviving English songs — passed down without written notation.

👗 Fashion, Status & Sumptuary Laws

👑 Wealthy Nobles
  • ✨ Velvet, silk, fur, fine linen
  • 🎨 Vivid dyes (red=love, yellow=anger, grey=sadness)
  • 👒 Steeple hats — nearly a metre tall in the 1480s
  • 👠 Poulaines — toes so pointed, tied back to ankle
🌾 Peasants
  • 🧶 Coarse wool or linen tunics
  • 🟫 Muted colours: grey, brown, dark green
  • 👞 Some owned no shoes at all
  • 🏠 Clothing was handmade and repaired endlessly

Sumptuary Laws were royal decrees forbidding lower classes from wearing fabrics, colours or styles associated with nobility. Their existence tells us something important: ordinary people were trying to imitate their social betters — and the ruling class found this threatening enough to stop it by law.

📖 The English Language After 1066

After the Conquest, English became the third most important language in England — below French and Latin. King Harold was the last monarch whose first language was English. By the 14th century, Chaucer was writing the Canterbury Tales in something very close to modern English.

Peasants ate hedgehog, squirrel, pike and eel without hesitation. No potatoes — they only reached England in the 1500s. Everyone drank weak ale — water was generally unsafe. A peasant's diet was probably healthier than many modern diets: no refined sugar, no processed food.

🔍 What is History?
& The Norman Conquest 1066

History is what historians argue happened, based on evidence they can find and interpret. The year 1066 is the most famous date in English history — and understanding WHY William won is where your study of medieval England begins.

📅 Key Date14 October 1066 — Battle of Hastings
🔑 ConceptChronology · Provenance · Change
📖 MethodWACA — Who, Audience, Context, Aim
🧠 HPL FocusCritical analysis · Curiosity

🧠 The WACA Framework

History is not about memorising dates — it is about asking questions, finding evidence, and building justified arguments. Apply WACA to every source before using it as evidence.

W
WHO made this? Their identity reveals potential bias.
A
Who is the AUDIENCE? Who was it made for?
C
What is the CONTEXT? When? What was happening?
A
What is the AIM? What was the creator trying to achieve?

👑 Three Men, One Crown — 1066

🏴 Harold Godwinson

England's most powerful earl. Crowned the day after Edward died. Strength: popular, experienced. Weakness: no royal blood.

🛡️ Harald Hardrada

King of Norway, nicknamed "the Ruthless" — most feared warrior in Europe. Strength: massive Viking army. Weakness: remote claim.

🏰 William of Normandy

Duke of Normandy. Claimed Edward promised him the throne. Strength: disciplined army, papal blessing. Nickname: "the Bastard".

📜 Primary Source A — Account of William at Hastings, c.1073
“Three horses were killed under him. Three times he leapt unafraid to the ground and killed the man who had killed his horse. With savage blows of his sword, he split shields, helmets and coats of chain mail.”
Written by William of Poitiers, c.1073 — a Norman writer who did NOT witness the battle. WACA: Who? When? Audience? Aim?
📅 Activity 1: Chronology Challenge
  • BC counts BACKWARDS — 100 BC is 500 years BEFORE 400 BC
  • Put in order: AD 1066, 350 BC, AD 1348, 55 BC, AD 1807, 776 BC
  • Create a timeline from 55 BC to 1215 with at least 8 events
⚔️ Activity 2: Three Claimants Investigation
  • Create a fact-file for all THREE claimants — claim, strength, weakness, fate
  • Who had the strongest LEGAL claim? Who had the strongest MILITARY claim?
  • 🥇 Gold: Was William's victory inevitable — or did Harold make critical errors?

☠️ Health, Medicine
& The Black Death

In the summer of 1348, a catastrophe arrived at the English port of Weymouth. Within two years, between a third and half of England's entire population was dead. Medieval medicine had no answer — yet from this catastrophe came the slow collapse of the feudal system.

📅 ArrivedJune 1348 — Weymouth
💀 Killed~30–50% of England
🦠 True CauseYersinia pestis (flea bacteria)
📅 Cause IdentifiedNot until 1894
📘 T&L⭐ HPLSDG 3SDG 10
🔊 Listen:

☠️ Symptoms & Medieval Responses

🦠 What the Black Death Did
  • 🔴 Large buboes in armpits and groin
  • ⚫ Black spots on arms or thighs
  • 🌡️ Severe fever, vomiting, extreme pain
  • ☠️ Death within 2–7 days
❌ Medieval Treatments (None Worked)
  • 🩸 Bloodletting
  • 🌿 Burning herbs against 'bad air'
  • 🙏 Prayer and fasting
  • 🐸 Strap a dried toad to the buboes
📜 Source D — Doctors at Paris University, 1348
“The long term cause is the position of the planets. It is also caused by evil smells which mix with the air and spread on the wind. When you breathe in the corrupted air you catch the plague.”
Report by doctors at Paris University, 1348.
📜 Source E — Jean de Venette, c.1348
“The disease was spread by contagion. If a healthy man visited a plague victim, he usually died himself.”
Written c.1348. Closest to the real cause!
☠️ Activity 1: WACA the Danse Macabre
  • The Danse Macabre above was created c.1493 — 145 years after the Black Death of 1348
  • Apply WACA: Who made it? For whom? When? With what aim? Does the 145-year gap matter?
  • Compare with a written account of 1348. What can each tell us that the other CANNOT?
💀 Activity 2: Royal Physician's Report
  • It is 1349. You are Edward III's royal physician. Write your official report:
  • THREE specific symptoms; THREE medieval theories for the cause; Assessment of THREE treatments
  • 🥉 Bronze: Describe two symptoms. 🥈 Silver: Explain cause and treatments. 🥇 Gold: Why were medieval doctors completely powerless? 💎 Platinum: Was the Black Death the most important event in medieval England?

🏰 Castles — Motte & Bailey
to Stone Behemoths

After Hastings, William the Conqueror faced a fundamental problem: how do you control a country whose entire population resents you? His answer was stone, timber, mortar and fear. Within months, Norman castles were rising across England — each one saying: We are here. We are permanent. Try to resist.

⏱️ Build TimeMotte & bailey: as little as 8 days
🏰 WallsTower of London: up to 2.5m thick
📊 Scale100+ castles by 1086
📅 Keyc.1078 — Tower of London begun
📘 T&L⭐ HPLSDG 16
🔊 Listen:

🏗️ Motte-and-Bailey Castles (1066 onwards)

By 1086, over 100 castles had been built across England. In Cambridge, 25 houses were demolished to make way for a castle. In Lincoln, the Normans pulled down over 150 houses. The English population had no choice.

🌲 Key Features
  • ⏱️ Built in just 7–14 days
  • 🌍 Motte = raised earthen mound
  • 🏡 Bailey = enclosed courtyard
  • 🔒 Wooden fences and walls
  • 🗼 Wooden keep on top of motte
⚠️ Weaknesses
  • 🔥 Could be set on fire
  • ⛏️ Could be smashed down
  • 💧 Wood would rot over time
  • 🪜 Walls could be climbed
  • 💡 So: build in stone from 1070!
📜 Source B — King John's Order, 1215
“Send to us with all speed by day and night, forty of the fattest pigs of the sort least good for eating so that we may bring fire underneath the castle.”
King John's written order during the Siege of Rochester Castle, 1215. Pig fat burns at 500°C — the entire south-east tower collapsed!
🏰 Activity 1: Motte-and-Bailey vs Stone Keep
  • Create a comparison table with at least SIX features for BOTH types of castle
  • Why did castle design change from wood to stone?
  • Why did barons continue building wooden castles instead of going straight to stone?
⚔️ Activity 2: Siege Diary — Rochester Castle 1215
  • Write a siege diary from the perspective of a soldier on King John's side
  • Include: why the siege began, weapons used, undermining plan, how 40 fat pigs won the day
  • 🥇 Gold: 'Castles were more important as symbols of power than as military fortifications.' How far do you agree?
